# Why a rhythm

The Getting Started series taught practices. This card is what the practices become — a durable rhythm of life that holds the four Connects in real time across decades, not weeks.

From Vol 2 of IJH: a man who has named his interior, has at least one person who knows his story, has the discipline of hearing scripture as the Spirit speaks, and is walking in what was prepared for him is in a different kind of life than a man who is doing none of these. The difference is not effort; it is rhythm. The man who has the rhythm has it because he set it on purpose and has returned to it many times. The man without it does not have the rhythm because nobody helped him build one.

This card is the rhythm. Print it. Stick it on your bathroom mirror or your dashboard or the inside of your journal cover. Customize the specifics. Sustain it across the interlude until Going Deeper begins, and then keep it across the seasons that follow.

## On returning

You will fall away from this rhythm. Most of us do, several times a year, sometimes for weeks at a time. When you fall away, do not start over from the beginning. Just begin again on the next available day.

Streaks are not the goal. Returning is the goal. The pattern of returning, over years, is what shapes a life. A man who has returned to the morning question fifty times after fifty falls is in a different relationship with God than a man who never started.

## On scope

The rhythm below is the structure. The specifics are yours. A retired grandfather and a high schooler will live this rhythm in different shapes — different times of day, different durations, different weekly partners, different monthly clearings. The structure is universal; the specifics are particular.

If a piece of the rhythm does not fit your season — you have a newborn and the morning question is competing with infant feedings, you are in finals and the monthly clearing time is impossible — do not abandon the whole rhythm. Keep what is doable; return to the rest when the season changes.

# Daily

Five to fifteen minutes a day. Two practices that take five minutes each. One optional practice that takes ten to fifteen minutes.

## The morning question (5 min)

## Practice

**Before you check your phone, sit somewhere quiet for five minutes with this question:**

***“Father, what are you up to today — and what do you want me to notice?”***

**Wait sixty seconds. Then write what comes — even if it’s nothing. Even ‘nothing came’ is a complete entry. The pattern recognition develops over weeks, not minutes.**

### Why the morning

Because the day has not yet shaped you. Because your phone has not yet shaped you. Because you are at your most receptive in the first ten minutes after waking, and the question gets to occupy the most-impressionable real estate of your day.

If your morning is genuinely impossible — small children, early commute, night-shift schedule — pick another moment that has the same quality. The first quiet ten minutes after the rush has settled. The walk into the office. The first cup of coffee at the desk. Not the literal morning, but the first available pre-formed quiet of the day.

### Common variations

- Two-minute version, when five is impossible. The question, sixty seconds of waiting, one written sentence. That is enough to keep the practice alive.
- Spoken version, in the car on the way to work or school. Same question. Listen as you drive. Speak the answer aloud at a stoplight if one comes.
- Walking version. Take the question out the door and onto a five-minute walk. Watch what surfaces.

## The evening journal note (1–2 min)

## Practice

**Before bed, write one sentence:**

***“Where did I see God today?”***

**That is the whole practice. One sentence. If you missed Him — write that. Honest noticing is what builds the faculty of seeing.**

### Why this practice in particular

Because most of us live retrospectively but never write retrospectively. We re-run the day in our heads and conclude it was good or bad without ever asking the more useful question: where did the Spirit show up, and did I notice?

From Vol 2: the Footprints exercise teaches the soul to read its own life. The evening note is the daily, micro-version of that exercise. Over years, it produces a person who notices God more easily in the present because he has had ten years of practice noticing Him in retrospect.

### If you cannot find Him

“I did not see Him today” is a complete and honest entry. Write it. Some days are like that. Across a year of evening notes, the days you missed Him will become the data you bring to a slower season — a retreat, a clearing time, a 1:1 with a trusted older Christian. The missing is itself useful information; the suppressed missing is not.

## (Optional) PROAPT one short passage (10–15 min)

## Practice

**Pray, Read, Observe, Apply, Pray-again, Tell. The six-step practice from Week 6.**

**Pick a short passage — a single verse, a paragraph, a few verses. Fewer than ten verses works best.**

**Daily on weekdays is plenty. Some seasons of life PROAPT will be the heart of your daily practice; some seasons it will be intermittent. Both are fine.**

### Choosing a passage

- If you are reading through a book of the Bible, pick the next short passage in your reading. Don’t over-think it; the passage will find you.
- If a sermon, a podcast, or a conversation surfaced a scripture this week — PROAPT that one tomorrow.
- If you are stuck on a question, pick a passage scripture has put near that question. Romans 8 if you are thinking about love. James 1 if you are thinking about wisdom. Psalm 139 if you are thinking about being known. The passage is part of the answer.
- If nothing comes — pick a Psalm. There are 150 of them. They will not run out.

### On the Tell step — because most people skip it

From Vol 2: of the six PROAPT steps, Tell is the most often skipped and the most important for long-term hearing. Speaking what you heard solidifies it; it also creates accountability to live what you heard.

Tell does not have to be public, formal, or theological. “Here is what I read this morning, and here is what I think it was saying to me” — to your spouse over coffee, to a friend in a text, to your weekly partner on the call — is enough. The point is to speak it aloud to one human being who is not yourself.

# Weekly

Two practices. The first is the most important practice in this entire card. The second is a sabbath rhythm of any shape.

## One specific person who knows what you are working on (60 min, weekly)

## Practice

**Once a week, for about an hour, you meet (in person, by phone, or by video) with one specific person who knows what you are working on spiritually right now.**

**Could be your spouse, your cohort co-participant from Getting Started, your Companion, a friend from Getting Started, an older Christian who has agreed to walk with you. One person.**

**The conversation has three parts: (1) What did I hear God doing this week? (2) What did I act on? (3) What did I miss?**

**You don’t have to follow this order. You don’t have to take an hour. The non-negotiable is: you speak your hearing aloud to one other person, and you do it weekly.**

### Why this practice is the most important on the card

From Vol 2 (the obedience-channel law in community form): a man who hears regularly but never reports his hearing to another person hears at a fraction of the depth of a man who knows he will report this week’s hearing next Tuesday. The reporting changes the listening. The accountability is not about behavior; it is about hearing.

This is also why isolated spirituality — even the disciplined kind — has limits. The Lone Christian Hero, hearing privately, acting privately, is a particularly American figure but not a New Testament one. The New Testament knows only Christians-in-community. The weekly partner is the smallest scriptural unit of that community.

### Choosing your weekly partner

- Someone who knows your story. The Getting Started cohort has put more than one such person in your life; one of them is the natural starting place.
- Someone who can be honest with you. A weekly partner who only encourages will, over time, become an echo chamber.
- Someone who is doing their own work. A partner who is not in their own ongoing formation will, gently, begin to shape the relationship around your work alone, which is not what this practice is.
- Not someone you supervise or who supervises you. Power differentials thin the conversation.
- Not someone you are dating, if you are dating. Different practice; different boundaries.

### If your weekly partner falls away

Some weekly partnerships last three months; some last thirty years. The summer team can help you find the next one if a weekly partner moves, takes a sabbatical, or just falls away. Email or call. Do not let the practice die because the partnership did.

## Sabbath rhythm (one day a week)

## Practice

**One day a week with a different shape from the other six. Less hurry. Less screen. More presence. More of whatever your soul actually feeds on — the long meal, the long walk, the long book, the long unmeasured time with people you love.**

**The shape of your sabbath is yours. The practice of having one is the discipline.**

### On the day you choose

Sunday for many Christians. Saturday for some, especially those who work Sundays. A Tuesday for those whose work week falls otherwise. The day is less important than the weekly recurrence and the differentness of the day.

### On what counts

The point is not productivity-free leisure. The point is a different kind of attention. A long meal with the family with phones in another room is sabbath. Three hours of golf alone with no phone is sabbath. Sitting on the porch with no agenda for an afternoon is sabbath. Catching up on email “just to clear the inbox” is not.

Most of us under-sabbath because we do not give ourselves permission to receive. The body learns over years that there is one day a week it does not have to perform; the soul learns slower, but it learns.

# Monthly

Two practices. About 90 minutes once a month each, though the second is more flexible.

## One clearing time (≈90 min)

## Practice

**Once a month, alone or with a trusted partner, set aside about 90 minutes for whatever heart maintenance is most needed in this season. Not therapy; formation work.**

**Could be: PROAPT a hard passage; a garden visit; an Any Doubts? round on a scripture you have been struggling with; a Footprints exercise on a specific season of your life; a Transformation Prayer Ministry session if you have a partner trained in TPM; a confession-and-restoration session if a place has been weighing.**

### Why monthly

Daily and weekly practices keep the channel open. Monthly is when you go in deeper than the regular rhythms can reach — a slower piece of soul-work that needs uninterrupted time and a willingness to sit with whatever surfaces.

Some seasons of life require this more often than monthly. During seasons of grief, transition, or significant spiritual warfare, weekly clearing time is appropriate. Monthly is the floor; you can do more.

### Where to do it

- Outdoors when possible. Many of the IJH practices (especially the Garden of Your Heart) work better in actual gardens, parks, walking trails.
- Somewhere your phone can be on airplane mode. Notifications interrupt the work.
- With a journal and a pen. Writing during clearing time consolidates what surfaces.

## Four-Connects review (≈30 min)

## Practice

**Once a month, sit with the four Connects and ask, honestly:**

**Self — where am I, honestly? What is my heart actually doing? Which of the four soils is dominant right now?**

**Others — who knows me right now? Is my weekly partnership genuinely working? Which of the four conditions am I weakest at offering this season?**

**God — how is the hearing going? Where is He surprising me? Where am I avoiding Him?**

**Mission — am I walking in what was prepared, or have I drifted into uphill-only? Where is the downhill mission flowing? Where is it blocked?**

### How to use the review

Some months the review will be brief and reassuring — most things are working, the rhythm is holding. Some months it will surface a place that needs attention: a weekly partnership that has thinned, a daily practice that has slipped, a Connect that has gone quiet. The review is the diagnostic that points the next month’s clearing time.

Once a year — typically January, or a birthday, or another anchor point — do a longer Four-Connects review. Two hours. Ideally with your weekly partner. The annual review reshapes the rhythm itself: maybe the time of day for the morning question changes; maybe the weekly partnership needs renewal or change; maybe a practice needs to come back that fell away two years ago.

# Seasonally (the deeper work)

Once or twice a year, something larger than the monthly rhythm can hold.

## A retreat or extended quiet time

## Practice

**Once or twice a year, an extended time — 24 hours, a weekend, a week — of going deeper than the regular practices can reach.**

**Could be a Wild at Heart Boot Camp, a contemplative retreat at a Christian retreat center, a personal solo retreat with PROAPT, journaling, and silence. Could be Going Deeper or another structured Fellowship of the Heart program.**

### Why seasonally

Some layers of soil and some species of knot are only accessible in extended uninterrupted time. The 90-minute monthly clearing time is enough for ordinary soul maintenance; the multi-day retreat is what reaches what the monthly cannot.

This is the practice most of us most often skip, because the calendar resists it and our spouses and children rightly notice when we leave. Plan it like a doctor’s appointment. Tell your weekly partner you are doing it; ask them to ask you about it afterward.

## A mission-clarity check

## Practice

**Once a year, sit with the mission question seriously. Am I still operating in the good works prepared for me? Has the season shifted? What does the Spirit seem to be calling me into next?**

**This is the seasonal version of the monthly Mission review. Slower, deeper, with more time for the actual answer to surface.**

# Your starter plan

If you are starting from this card, do not try to install the entire rhythm in week one. Most people who try to install everything at once collapse the rhythm within four weeks.

Here is the order I recommend:

## Weeks 1–2

- The morning question, daily.
- The evening journal note, daily.

Just these two. Five minutes plus one minute. Build the habit before adding anything.

## Weeks 3–4

- Continue the daily practice.
- Start the weekly partnership. Make the first call this week.

## Month 2

- Continue the daily and weekly practices.
- Add the sabbath rhythm. One day a week with different shape.
- Schedule your first monthly clearing time on the calendar before the month is over.

## Month 3

- Continue daily, weekly, sabbath, and monthly.
- Optional: PROAPT one short passage daily on weekdays.
- Consider whether a seasonal retreat fits this year.

## After three months

If the rhythm is genuinely held — not perfectly, but genuinely — you are now in a different relationship with God than you were ninety days ago. You may not be able to articulate the difference. The people closest to you usually can.

The next year is about deepening, not adding. The same rhythm — lived more honestly, with one or two specific places where you have started to notice things you did not notice before.

# A final word

This rhythm is not the goal. The goal is a life in which you know God more deeply, are known by people who can love you well, walk in the work He prepared for you, and finish well. The rhythm is the structure that makes that life more likely.

From Vol 1 of IJH: the obedience channel opens not when we know everything, but when we do the next thing we have heard. The rhythm above is just an organized way of doing the next thing, every day, for the rest of your life.
