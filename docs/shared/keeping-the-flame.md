*Shared materials — all three series*

**Keeping the Flame**

*For every Companion, adult or FC1 — how a calling survives its own success*

*Based on the Intentional Journey of the Heart (IJH), Volumes 1–6, and* A Church Prepared for Revival

*John G. Tittle • Pilot edition, July 2026*

# Why this card exists

There is a kind of falling-away that never misses a session. The letter to Ephesus describes it exactly: works, toil, patient endurance — all commended — "but I have this against you, that you have abandoned the love you had at first" (Rev. 2:4). A Companion can drift with the room still running beautifully, because leader-drift almost always looks like *improvement*: prep gets faster, sessions get smoother, nothing goes wrong. Polish is what is left when hunger leaves.

This is the occupational hazard of handling holy things regularly. Malachi's priests were still offering the sacrifices when they started saying "what a weariness this is" (Mal. 1:13). What was fire the first time becomes procedure by the tenth — not because anyone sinned, but because that is what fire does when nobody tends it. Which is why Scripture's instruction assumes the danger is universal: "fan into flame the gift of God" (2 Tim. 1:6). Flames bank down by default. Rekindling is deliberate.

This card is the tending. The [Signs Card](signs-card.md) is for the season when the practices go dry; this card is for the subtler season when the practices go *fine* — and the wanting quietly retires.

# The one question, leaders' edition

The Signs Card's sorting question works on Companions unchanged: **which way does the desire point?**

A dry season *aches* toward God — the distance hurts, the wanting is alive. That is the tide, and the Signs Card handles it; dryness is not drift. Satisfaction is different: the role tastes fine *without* the ache. The sessions satisfy. The competence satisfies. The calling stopped hurting — and you did not notice, because nothing was wrong.

# The Companion's tells — a self-check

No one else can run this check on you, and no one should. Once a cycle, alone before God, honestly:

- I prepare from memory now, and prayer has quietly fallen out of the preparation.
- Sessions run well — and I have stopped expecting God to actually *do* anything tonight.
- I talk about the practices more than I privately do them.
- When someone in my circle has a breakthrough, what stirs in me is professional satisfaction, not worship.
- I have not told anything costly, to anyone, in months.
- I cannot remember the last time I was *led* — the last time I received a practice as a sheep instead of running it as a shepherd.

One of these, in a tired season, is a tired season. Three or more, holding steady while everything runs smoothly — that is the flame banking down.

# The five defenses

**1. Rebuild your own Rhythm Card every cycle — in your own handwriting.** The first competency of a Companion is that your own formation is real and ongoing, not technique over an empty interior. So do what the cohort does in Week 13: a fresh card, honest, not idealized — *what am I actually practicing now?* A leader running last year's rhythm from memory is already coasting.

**2. Stay a sheep somewhere.** Every shepherd needs a room where they are only a member — a standing cadence, outside every line of consequence, where *you* tell the costly things. Being under authority is not the same as having such a room; you can be covered and still have nowhere to be honest. If you lead and have no room like this, finding one outranks everything else on this card. Drift is caught in that room months before your cohort could see it — because the first thing satisfaction kills is the leader's own costly telling.

**3. Always carry one unmastered assignment.** A calling is fed by load, and satisfaction is often just the symptom of insufficient current challenge — you mastered what you were given and nobody handed you the next edge. Caleb's cure at eighty-five was not rest; it was asking for the giants' mountain (Josh. 14:12). The ladder is already built: leading a new block, adapting the series, inviting others, forming the *next* Companion. That last one is the surest rekindler there is — "entrust to faithful men, who will be able to teach others also" (2 Tim. 2:2) — because nothing relights a plateaued teacher like a hungry student.

**4. Keep receiving what you lead.** The teen leaders' third rule — *lead only a block you have first received* — has a maintenance clause for all of us: keep receiving it. PROAPT as a hearer, not as the one running it. The Garden with someone else guiding. A Companion who has not been led in a year is running on stored fuel, and stored fuel runs out quietly.

**5. Run the three vital signs on yourself.** The same [three questions](three-vital-signs.md) the team asks about the room, in the first person: Have I told anything costly lately — and where? When load arrived this season, what did I actually do — hope, or professional management? When someone went quiet on me, did I go after them with an ache, or with a checklist?

# If the flame has banked — the recovery protocol

Ephesus was given three steps, in order (Rev. 2:5): **remember, repent, and do the works you did at first.**

**Remember** — retell your calling story. Why you said yes. What the room felt like the first time you led and God showed up. Tell it out loud, to your circle or your covering. This is not nostalgia — nostalgia stops at remembering; Ephesus's remembering is fuel for the next step.

**Repent** — name the drift without shame. This card exists so that naming it is ordinary maintenance, not a scandal. The Father who runs to meet returning sons does not scold returning shepherds.

**Do the first works** — go back to being a participant. Take a season of receiving. Sit in someone else's circle. Do the practices privately, small and daily, the way you did before anyone was watching.

# What is NOT drift

A fallow season with the desire intact is sabbath, and sabbath is commanded. Rest after a long labor, grief that needs its winter, a body that needs sleep — none of these are the flame failing; the Signs Card sorts them. The question is never *how much are you doing*. It is only ever *which way does the desire point*.

# The promise

The reward for faithfulness, in Jesus' own telling, is not retirement — it is *more*: "You have been faithful over a little; I will set you over much. Enter into the joy of your master" (Matt. 25:21). The calling widens. You were made for the next mountain too.

And for the long arc — for the Companion who has built much and wonders what is left — the Psalter has already written the assignment that never expires: "So even to old age and gray hairs, O God, do not forsake me, until I proclaim Your might to another generation, Your power to all those to come" (Ps. 71:18). Those who are planted in the house of the LORD "still bear fruit in old age; they are ever full of sap and green" (Ps. 92:13–14). There is always another generation in the room. That is the flame's food, to the very end.

# How this card is used

- Walked together at the **first Leader Lab of every serving cycle** — calling stories retold, Rhythm Cards rebuilt, each Companion's unmastered assignment for the cycle named, each Companion's sheep-room confirmed.
- Revisited **personally, once a cycle**, for the self-check — alone, before God, unhurried.
- Never used as a checklist on someone else. This card is a mirror, not a lever; handing it to another Companion with raised eyebrows is exactly the instrument the Measurement Covenant forbids. If you are worried about a brother, sit with him — don't score him.
