**The Three Vital Signs**

*The Companion team's weekly lens — three readings, taken in ordinary time, that tell you how a body is actually doing*

*Based on the Intentional Journey of the Heart (IJH), Volumes 1–6, and* A Church Prepared for Revival

*John G. Tittle • Pilot edition, July 2026*

# Why this card exists

A fellowship can drown in measurement. The assessment plan behind this pilot has surveys, journals, observation notes, and a research framework several volumes deep — and a Companion in an ordinary Tuesday session needs almost none of it. A doctor takes four vital signs at every visit and orders the deeper tests only when one of them is off. This card is the Companion's version: **three signs, watchable in an ordinary session, with no form in anyone's hand.** Everything else in the assessment plan is the deeper workup — used rarely, gently, and only when a vital sign says to look closer.

The three signs are not three separate things. Underneath them is one thing: **confidence in God in the middle of suffering, noise, and even disaster.** Hope that is anchored in Him, not in outcomes — the "but if not" of the three men at the furnace (Dan. 3:17–18), who knew God was able to deliver them and had already decided how they would stand if He didn't. That kind of hope cannot be read on a survey taken on a calm night, because it only shows itself under load. So the signs are read where load actually arrives: in ordinary weeks, with their ordinary interruptions, emergencies, and griefs.

# Sign 1 — Costly Telling

**What it is.** Someone tells the group what God said and what they did about it — or tells something true that costs them to say.

**Why it reads so much at once.** Telling is obedience made visible — it is the sixth step of PROAPT for a reason. And telling is also the room's safety gauge, because it is the first thing to stop when a room stops being safe. People fall silent long before they walk away. One sign, two readings: *is God being heard and obeyed here, and is it safe to say so?*

**What to watch.** Does telling happen at all? Does it ever cost anything, or is it all polished? Does it come from more than the same three people? And watch what the room does with a costly tell — receiving it well is the sign at full strength; rushing to fix it is the sign starting to fail.

**The counterfeit.** Performed testimony — frequent, fluent, and free. Count the cost, not the occurrences.

# Sign 2 — Response to Load

**What it is.** What a person, or the room, does when load arrives — an interruption, an emergency, hard news, real suffering.

**What to watch.** The first ten seconds after a disruption. Does the room absorb it or panic? Do people move toward the trouble or look to the leader to handle it? When you ask someone *why* — and then ask why again — does the answer deepen, or does it scatter? A settled second answer is experience speaking; a scattered one is a script running out.

**Tears are not a bad reading.** A room that grieves openly and hopes anyway is the sign at its strongest, not its weakest — "sorrowful, yet always rejoicing" (2 Cor. 6:10). What reads low is not grief but despair — and not fear felt, but fear handed around as the verdict.

**The counterfeit.** Presumption — calm that rests on a guaranteed outcome rather than on God. The gentle test, where expectancy runs high: *"and what if it doesn't turn out?"* Anchored hope has already been to that question. Presumption hasn't, and it shows.

# Sign 3 — The Sought Stray

**What it is.** When someone goes quiet or goes missing — how long before anyone notices, and who goes after them.

**Why it earns its place.** Jesus made love-between-members the one sign He said outsiders could read (John 13:35), and the stray no one sought is the first symptom on Scripture's own list of a failing flock (Ezek. 34:4). It is also the cheapest reading on this card: it is nearly a yes-or-no.

**What to watch.** Time-to-notice. Whether noticing turns into pursuit or into a mention at the debrief. Whether pursuit comes only from the Companions, or from the body itself — the body noticing is a stronger reading than the shepherds noticing.

**The counterfeit.** Pursuit that smothers. Going after someone is love; refusing them room to breathe is not. The Path Home Card is the model: the door held open, not the door forced.

# How to read the signs

- **Three lenses on every reading:** where is it now · which way is it moving · what is promised. No reading is finished at the bad news; even the hardest of the seven letters carries the largest promise (Rev. 3:21). A Companion who reads a low sign and cannot say what to tend next hasn't finished the reading.
- **A body's reading is its lowest steady sign, not its average.** Same rule as the Group Level taxonomy: three strong tellers do not make a telling room.
- **Watch — never score.** No numbers on people, no rankings, no comparisons. The signs live in the Companion team's weekly debrief, under the [Measurement Covenant](measurement-covenant.md), and nowhere else.
- **When a sign goes gray, look up before you look around.** The first question is never *which participant is the problem* — it is *what have we as Companions not seen, or seen and not tended?* Only after that does the deeper workup come out, gently: a conversation, the [Interrogating Reality Card](interrogating-reality-card.md), the covering.
- **A dry season is not a failing sign.** The [Signs Card](signs-card.md) sorts the kinds of dry; the tide going out is not the anchor dragging. What this card watches for is not dryness but despair — and the difference is which way the desire points.

# The weekly debrief — the practical use

Open the Companion team's 30-minute debrief with the three signs, in order:

1. **Who told something costly this week — and what did the room do with it?**
2. **Where did load arrive this week — and what did we see in the first ten seconds?**
3. **Who has gone quiet — and who is going after them?**

Then the usual notes. Ten minutes, three questions, every week. That rhythm — not any instrument — is the pilot's real assessment plan.
