*Shared materials — all three series*

**Path Home Card**

*Confession, lament, return — the written way back, for anyone who has been away*

*Based on the Intentional Journey of the Heart (IJH), Volumes 1–6*

*John G. Tittle • Pilot edition, July 2026*

# Why this card exists

Every formation journey worth taking has people who fall away from it. Some drift (the [Signs Card](signs-card.md) names that quiet fade). Some burn out. Some blow it — really blow it — and conclude the room they used to sit in would not want them back. And most churches, without ever meaning to, leave the way back unwritten: there are ten weeks of material for starting the journey and not one page for *returning* to it. So the returner has to improvise the hardest walk of their life, alone, guessing at the reception.

Not here. This card is the written door. If you have been away — a month, a year, a decade; quietly or spectacularly — this is the way back in, and you should know before you take the first step how it ends: it ends with a feast, not a hearing. "But while he was still a long way off, his father saw him and felt compassion, and ran and embraced him and kissed him" (Luke 15:20, ESV). The father runs. That is the reception. Everything below is just the road.

# Step one — come as you are, and check which voice brought you

The way back begins before any words: it begins with turning toward home at all. And at that first turn, learn to tell two voices apart, because both of them will be talking.

**Conviction** is the Spirit's voice, and it draws you *toward* home: specific about what happened, hopeful about what comes next, and it gets lighter the closer you get. **Shame** is the other voice, and it drives you *away*: vague and global ("you're just like this"), hopeless, and it gets louder the closer you get to the people who love you. Scripture puts the difference in one verse: "godly grief produces a repentance that leads to salvation without regret, whereas worldly grief produces death" (2 Cor. 7:10, ESV). Follow the grief that leads somewhere. The voice telling you the door is barred is not the doorkeeper's voice; the doorkeeper left the door unlatched and the light on.

# Step two — tell it straight, to God and to one person

Confession is not groveling; it is *telling the truth out loud*, and it is the fastest honest step on the road. To God first, in plain words — no polish, no spin: "I said, 'I will confess my transgressions to the LORD,' and you forgave the iniquity of my sin" (Psalm 32:5, ESV). His side of that transaction is already promised: "If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just to forgive us our sins" (1 John 1:9, ESV).

Then to **one trusted person** — a Companion, a friend from the old cohort, anyone from this fellowship who knows how to hear a soul. One is enough. This is a *sovereign share*: you choose the person, you choose how much, and nobody in this fellowship will ever require a public confession or hand your story to anyone else. But do not skip the human half. Sin loves the dark, and a secret return has a way of not lasting; the thing said out loud to one kind face loses half its weight on the spot (James 5:16).

# Step three — grieve what needs grieving

Some returns carry real losses — time, trust, a relationship, a season of life that is simply gone. The world says move on; Scripture says something better: *lament*. God's own songbook devotes a third of its pages to griefs sung out loud in His direction, which settles the question of whether He can bear to hear yours. Borrow the psalmists' words when you have none (Psalm 51 has walked this exact road; so has Psalm 130: "Out of the depths I cry to you, O LORD"). Take the time the grief actually takes. Lament is not the opposite of faith; it is faith, with tears in its voice, refusing to leave the conversation.

And know the promise standing at the end of it: "Those who sow in tears shall reap with shouts of joy" (Psalm 126:5, ESV).

# Step four — return to the table

Now the practical part, and notice how unglamorous it is: you just start again, small. The [Rhythm Card](rhythm-card.md)'s own rule was written for this moment — *do not start over from the beginning; begin again on the next available day.* Five dry minutes tomorrow morning is a full return. Come back to the room — the cohort, the group, the table. You do not need to earn the seat; it was set before you got there. "Return to me, and I will return to you, says the LORD of hosts" (Malachi 3:7, ESV) — and He has never once been the slower party to that exchange: "rend your hearts and not your garments. Return to the LORD your God, for he is gracious and merciful, slow to anger, and abounding in steadfast love" (Joel 2:13, ESV).

Trust regrows at the speed of soil, not the speed of apology — with yourself and sometimes with others — and that is all right. Roots grow at the speed of roots. The return is real from the first day; it just gets *visible* over a season.

# Step five — let it be celebrated

This step is not optional, and it is the one churches most often skip. In the Lord's own story of return, the ending is not a probation meeting; it is music and a feast, and the father defends the party to the older brother in the strongest words in the parable: "It was *fitting* to celebrate and be glad, for this your brother was dead, and is alive; he was lost, and is found" (Luke 15:32, ESV). Heaven itself throws this party: "there is joy before the angels of God over one sinner who repents" (Luke 15:10, ESV).

So when someone comes home to this fellowship, we celebrate — a meal, a song, a blessing spoken over them, whatever fits the person (celebration, like confession, is sovereign: the returner chooses how public). A fellowship that only ever sings on the way out and never for the way back has told its strugglers, without meaning to, that leaving is more interesting than returning. Not here. Here, returning is the most celebrated move a person can make.

# For Companions — the posture of the house

When someone returns, the whole card above is theirs. This last section is yours.

- **Your role is the father's posture, not the older brother's.** The father ran, embraced first, and asked for no accounting before the robe. The older brother did the math. Every returner meets both instincts in a congregation; make sure they meet the father's *first*, and in you.
- **Never require the story.** Welcome does not run on disclosure. The returner shares what they choose, with whom they choose, at the pace they choose — and what they share travels nowhere without their consent, exactly as the [Measurement Covenant](measurement-covenant.md) promises.
- **Watch the door you came through.** The strongest welcome-home teams are staffed by people who have themselves returned from somewhere. If that is you, your story — shared at your own choosing — is the proof on legs that the door really does open.
- **Restoration to *serving* is a road, not a moment.** Returning to the table is immediate and unconditional. Returning to leading, where that applies, follows the same patient, overseen road the fellowship's oversight structure lays out — and that patience is a kindness to the returner, not a punishment.

The lost sheep gets carried home on shoulders, "rejoicing" (Luke 15:5). That is the whole spirit of this card. The door is not locked. The light is on. Come home.
