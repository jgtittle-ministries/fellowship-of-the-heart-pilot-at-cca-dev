# How to use this list

This list is curated for parents, teens, and other family members who are walking the four Connects work — not for academic study. Volume 5 of the IJH project contains a much fuller bibliography for those who want the deeper sources; this list is the on-ramp.

The books here are organized by Connect, with a small fifth section on integration and rhythm. Each entry has a brief annotation, a tag for who it is for, and a note about when in the journey it is most useful. Not every book is for every reader; pick what fits your season.

## On reading well

- Pick one book at a time. Two books in parallel is normal; six books in parallel is a way to read nothing well.
- Read with a pen. Underline what lands. Write a sentence in the margin when something disagrees with you.
- Bring what you read to your weekly partner. “Here is a thing I read this week that has stayed with me.” Ten minutes of conversation with one person about a book is worth more than five chapters read alone.
- Skip what does not land. Return to it in five years. Some books are right for the wrong season; the same book can be exactly right later.
- If a book unsettles you, that is data. Do not abandon a book just because it is uncomfortable; sometimes the discomfort is the work. But also do not finish a book that is producing harm.

## Audience tags

**Teen — accessible to senior teens (15–18); some are also for thoughtful junior teens.**

**Parent — written for adult readers; some require some life experience to land.**

**Both — readable across ages; often the books that produce the best parent-teen conversations.**

**Advanced — deeper, denser, or theologically heavier; for readers who want the systematic version.**

# Self — the soil, the story, and the wound

Books for the work of Connecting with Self — the diagnostic and therapeutic work of understanding your own interior.

**Wild at Heart: Discovering the Secret of a Man's Soul**

*John Eldredge* • 2001

**Audience:** Teen and Parent (men, primarily). Written for men but read by many women too. **Use for:** After Week 3 (telling your story) and Week 9 (mission). The book to give the senior teen son or husband whose heart has gone quiet.

Eldredge’s central argument — that the masculine heart has design-level desires (a battle to fight, an adventure to live, a beauty to rescue) that are wounded by the world and contested by an enemy — is the source material for Vol 2’s Identity Blockage Law. The book is not difficult reading, but it is searching. Many men recognize themselves in it before they can argue about it. Recommended without reservation as a companion to the more systematic IJH framework.

**Captivating: Unveiling the Mystery of a Woman's Soul**

*John and Stasi Eldredge* • 2005

**Audience:** Teen and Parent (women, primarily). **Use for:** The companion to Wild at Heart for women. Use after Week 3 or Week 9.

Stasi Eldredge’s account of the feminine heart, written with John, working out the design desires (to be romanced, to play an irreplaceable role in a great adventure, to unveil beauty) that mirror the masculine ones. The same caution: the book is searching, and many women recognize the territory before they have argued about it.

**Waking the Dead: The Glory of a Heart Fully Alive**

*John Eldredge* • 2003

**Audience:** Parent. Some senior teens. **Use for:** After Wild at Heart. The book that goes deeper into the spiritual-warfare dimension of the wound and the false self.

Vol 2’s Exploration 2B draws specifically on chapters 9–11 of Waking the Dead for the enemy’s strategy against the heart. The book moves from diagnostic (what is wrong) to therapeutic (the recovery of the heart in Christ). The third quarter, on the four streams of formation, is one of the most useful overviews of how the historical traditions of Christian formation actually fit together.

**Restoring the Christian Soul Through Healing Prayer**

*Leanne Payne* • 1991

**Audience:** Parent. Advanced. **Use for:** After the Garden of Your Heart (Wk 7) has surfaced something. For readers who want to understand the inner-healing tradition deeply.

Payne’s work is the most theologically rigorous account of inner-healing prayer in the Protestant tradition. She is unsparing about the ways the wound and the false self distort relationship with God, and she is precise about how prayer-as-encounter (not prayer-as-petition) restores the soul. Denser than Eldredge; rewards the work.

**The Practice of the Presence of God**

*Brother Lawrence* • c. 1692, modern editions widely available

**Audience:** Both. Surprisingly accessible. **Use for:** The morning question and the evening note. The shortest book on the list, and possibly the most formative.

A 17th-century Carmelite cook’s short letters and reflections on doing the dishes in the awareness of God. Almost nothing on the list is more practical for the daily rhythm. Read slowly; one or two pages a day. The Helms/Edmonson edition (Paraclete Press, 1985) is the most readable.

# Others — community, friendship, and the container

Books for the work of Connecting with Others — the architecture of real friendship and small-group community.

**Celebration of Discipline: The Path to Spiritual Growth**

*Richard Foster* • 1978, 3rd ed. 1998

**Audience:** Both. **Use for:** After Getting Started, as a structural overview of the disciplines we touched lightly. The chapters on confession, worship, and guidance map directly onto the four Connects work.

The book that introduced two generations of Protestant Christians to the historical disciplines of the church (inward, outward, corporate). Foster’s genius is making practices accessible without flattening their depth. Read one chapter a week, with a friend or weekly partner who is reading the same chapter.

**Streams of Living Water: Celebrating the Great Traditions of Christian Faith**

*Richard Foster* • 1998

**Audience:** Parent. Advanced. **Use for:** For the parent or senior teen who wants to understand why the church has not always looked the way their tradition makes it look.

Foster’s map of six streams of Christian formation — contemplative, holiness, charismatic, social-justice, evangelical, incarnational — each rooted in scripture and embodied across history. The book that makes ecumenical generosity possible without theological mush. Especially useful for senior teens who are starting to ask why their tradition is shaped the way it is.

**Spiritual Theology: A Systematic Study of the Christian Life**

*Simon Chan* • 1998

**Audience:** Advanced. **Use for:** After Getting Started, for the reader who wants the systematic account that grounds the practical work.

Chan’s work is one of the best one-volume systematic accounts of Christian formation written in the last fifty years. He integrates the Asian Pentecostal tradition with the Western academic theology of formation. Denser than Foster; for readers who want the framework behind the practice.

**Group Dynamics for Teams**

*Daniel Levi and David Askay* • 6th ed. 2021

**Audience:** Parent. Cohort Companions. **Use for:** For anyone leading a group through this work. Not specifically Christian, but the social-science work it summarizes is essential for understanding why container conditions matter.

A textbook on small-group dynamics that explains, in language the IJH project does not duplicate, why the four conditions of a real container (Vol 2 Exp 8) work the way they do. Useful for Companions and for parents who are thinking about why some friend groups go deep and others stay shallow.

# God — hearing, prayer, and the Spirit

Books for the work of Connecting with God — the practical formation of the hearing faculty.

**Decision Making and the Will of God**

*Garry Friesen with J. Robin Maxson* • 1980, expanded ed. 2004

**Audience:** Both. Especially senior teens facing college decisions. **Use for:** When a teen or parent asks “how do I know God’s will” and means it as a real question.

Friesen’s argument — that the popular evangelical doctrine of an individual divine “perfect will” for every decision is not actually what scripture teaches — lands hard for senior teens raised on “surrender to God’s will” language. The book recovers the older, sturdier biblical framework of wisdom, scripture, and counsel. Read with care; some chapters are dense. The expanded edition has helpful Q&A material.

**Desiring the Kingdom: Worship, Worldview, and Cultural Formation**

*James K. A. Smith* • 2009

**Audience:** Parent. Advanced. **Use for:** For the reader who wants to understand why what we love (not what we know) shapes us.

Smith’s argument — that human beings are formed not primarily by what we know but by what we love, and that what we love is shaped by the rituals and liturgies we participate in (whether at church, at the mall, on social media, or at a sports event) — is one of the most important insights for parents thinking about the formation of teens. The book is academic but readable. Vol 5 of IJH cites Smith specifically on the affective domain of formation.

**The Way of Spiritual Direction**

*Francis Kelly Nemeck and Marie Theresa Coombs* • 1985

**Audience:** Parent. Advanced. **Use for:** For anyone seeking a spiritual director, or for the reader who wants to understand what genuine direction looks like.

From Vol 2 of IJH: spiritual direction is “a lost art in the Protestant community.” Nemeck and Coombs’ work, from within the Carmelite tradition, is the best practical introduction to what spiritual direction is, what it is not (it is not counseling, not advice-giving, not pastoral care), and how to find a qualified director. Especially valuable for parents in their forties or fifties who want a deeper companion for the next stretch of their journey.

**The Spiritual Exercises**

*Ignatius of Loyola* • c. 1548; modern editions widely available

**Audience:** Advanced. Best done with a spiritual director. **Use for:** After several years of the rhythm. Not a beginner’s book.

The 16th-century Jesuit handbook for a 30-day silent retreat. Most lay people do the Spiritual Exercises in the form called the “Exercises in everyday life” — a 9-month version that fits around ordinary work and family. Read about the Exercises before doing them; the book itself is a director’s manual, not a self-help text. The Examen practice (a daily 5-step review) can be drawn from the Exercises and used independently — see Vol 2 Exp 7 of IJH.

**The Spiritual Man**

*Watchman Nee* • 1928, modern editions

**Audience:** Advanced. **Use for:** For the reader who wants the tripartite anthropology (spirit / soul / body) that grounds the IJH nested-person structure.

Nee’s magnum opus on Christian spirituality. Long, demanding, occasionally idiosyncratic. The closest historical precedent for the nested-person model in Vol 1 of IJH. Many readers find The Release of the Spirit a more accessible entry point to the same core ideas. Read with discernment; Nee’s ecclesiology is not what we commend, but his anthropology is profound.

# Mission — calling, work, and what was prepared

Books for the work of Connecting with Mission — the discovery and walking of what was prepared for you.

**Gateways to God**

*Dmitri Bilgere* • 2013, multiple later editions

**Audience:** Parent. Advanced. **Use for:** After Week 9 (mission), for readers who want the deeper work behind the uphill / downhill distinction.

Bilgere’s work is integrated into Vol 2 of IJH on two specific points: the uphill / downhill mission distinction (Vol 2 Exp 5) and the Any Doubts? exercise (Vol 2 Exp 7). The book is published outside the standard Christian publishing channels and is best read with discernment, but the practical work is some of the most powerful in the IJH lineage. The author writes from a Christian framework but engages secular and ecumenical sources.

**The Screwtape Letters**

*C. S. Lewis* • 1942

**Audience:** Both. Excellent for senior teens. **Use for:** For understanding the specific ways the enemy contests calling. Not technically a mission book; structurally one of the best.

Lewis’s short epistolary novel — letters from a senior demon to a junior demon training in the corruption of a young man’s soul — is a master class in how shadow mission operates. The book’s laughter is the medium of its seriousness. A senior teen who reads this carefully will recognize patterns in their own life that the IJH framework names more abstractly.

**The Last Battle**

*C. S. Lewis* • 1956

**Audience:** Both. Even more useful if read alongside the rest of the Narnia series. **Use for:** For the eschatological imagination that grounds the whole project.

The seventh and final Narnia book. Read for its specific ending: the moment Lewis describes as “further up and further in,” and the description of Aslan’s country as “the real Narnia.” Vol 5 of IJH cites Lewis on the eschatological orientation that makes the journey meaningful. Senior teens who grew up with Narnia often read this book differently as adults; the second reading is part of the work.

# Integration and rhythm — the long view

Books for the long-term shaping of a life of formation, beyond any single Connect.

**Foundations of Spiritual Formation**

*Edited by Paul Pettit (multiple contributors including Bramer, Bock, Morrow, Shields, Johnston)* • 2008

**Audience:** Advanced. Reference work. **Use for:** For readers who want a survey of the academic field of spiritual formation as it stands today.

An evangelical academic anthology covering the history, theology, and practice of formation. Vol 5 of IJH cites several chapters specifically: Bramer on the affective domain, Shields on preaching as formation, Bock on the New Testament community. Best read selectively rather than cover-to-cover. The book is useful for serious Companions and for parents who want to engage the field at a higher level.

**Spirituality of the Psalms**

*Walter Brueggemann* • 2002

**Audience:** Both. Especially useful for parents. **Use for:** For the daily practice of PROAPT on the Psalter, and for the seasons when scripture has gone flat.

Brueggemann’s short book reorganizes the Psalms into three categories — orientation, disorientation, new orientation — and shows how the Psalter teaches the soul to pray honestly across every season of life. Not an academic book; a pastor’s book. The chapter on disorientation is one of the best things written in English on praying through grief. Recommended for any reader for whom “the Psalms always sound the same” has become true; the book changes that.

**Move: What 1,000 Churches Reveal about Spiritual Growth**

*Greg Hawkins and Cally Parkinson* • 2011

**Audience:** Parent. Advanced. **Use for:** For parents who are thinking about why some churches form their members and some do not.

The Willow Creek REVEAL study, in book form. The empirical finding that surprised the authors — that programs do not produce growth past a certain stage; only personal disciplines and one-on-one relationships do — is foundational for understanding why the IJH framework emphasizes weekly partnerships and personal practices over additional programming. Useful especially for parents serving on church leadership.

# A last word

This list is not exhaustive and is not authoritative. The Vol 5 IJH bibliography contains many more entries; some of those will be more useful to you than what is here. The goal of this short list is to give a starting place — not to substitute for your own discernment about what you should read next.

If you read three books from this list well — with a pen, with a friend, with prayer — you will be in a different place by next year than you are today. If you read fifteen books from this list quickly, you will mostly remember that you read them.

Pick what fits the season. Read it slowly. Bring it to someone who knows you. Return to the list when the next season starts.

*— John*
