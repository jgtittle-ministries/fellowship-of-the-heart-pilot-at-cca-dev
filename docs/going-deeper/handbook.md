**Section 1 — The Series at a Glance**

**Title and theme**

“Going Deeper.” The title is purposefully simple. Getting Started did the introduction; Going Deeper engages into each of the four Connects with the diagnostic and therapeutic work of IJH Volume 2.

**The big idea**

From IJH Volume 2: most of us, by the time we are sitting in any kind of small-group community, are operating in compliance and surface friendship. The four Connects — Self, Others, God, Mission — are not slogans; they are diagnostic categories. Each has its own depth, its own characteristic blockages, and its own specific tools. Getting Started named them; Going Deeper engages them as practical formation.

Vol 2 names a five-stage progression for hearing God — Receiving, Responding, Valuing, Organization, Characterization. Most Getting Started cohort participants completed Getting Started somewhere in the Stage 2-to-3 transition: still extrinsically motivated, but beginning to value the work for its own sake. Going Deeper aims explicitly at the Stage 2-to-3 to Stage 3-to-4 transitions. Not by pushing harder; by offering tools that target what is actually blocking the next stage.

**Series outcomes**

By the end of Going Deeper, a participant who walked the full twelve weeks should be able to:

**•** Diagnose the four kinds of emotional knot in their own interior — grief, shame, fear, anger — and name which is operating in a specific situation.

**•** Identify and bring to Jesus a specific lie-at-the-root that has been shaping a part of their life, with at least one experience of Spirit-delivered truth replacing it.

**•** Practice confession-and-restoration in community — not as ritual, but as a known and useful tool with one trusted partner.

**•** Participate in corporate listening prayer for one of their cohort members and be the recipient of the same.

**•** Articulate their own primary mission with more specificity than at the close of Getting Started, and have at least one specific shadow-mission they are watching for in themselves.

**•** Carry the architecture of Acts 13-style calling discernment, having either been the Discerner themselves or having served the cohort as one of the listeners during the Wk 10 work.

**•** Honestly assess where their cohort actually landed on the five-level group taxonomy — not where they wished it had landed.

**•** Be ready to enter Going Out (the Going Out series) on truthful foundations — OR honestly discern that their season of formation work is complete for now.

**What Getting Started did, and what Going Deeper does**

Getting Started ran the four Connects sequentially — each Connect introduced in turn, each given some experiential time, with the architecture of cohort circles, container conditions, and family commissioning. Getting Started used Vol 1’s tools (PROAPT for hearing, Tell Your Story for safety, the Aaronic blessing as the closing) at the level of introduction. It built the container, established the cohort circles, and ended with a family commissioning that made the Getting Started’s work public.

What Getting Started did not do: engage Vol 2’s actual diagnostic-therapeutic-developmental architecture. Getting Started borrowed from Part II (the four Connects sequence) and Part III (the container), but it did not run Part I (the diagnostic work — the four soils, the knot taxonomy, the wound and false self, the lie at the root). It did not do corporate listening prayer in the Acts 13 mode. It did not name shadow mission as a discipline.

Going Deeper engages those things. The four soils as working diagnostic, not metaphor. The four knot types and the lie at the root, named in each participant. Confession-and-restoration in standing pairs. Corporate listening prayer for one Bringer (Wk 7) and for the cohort itself (Wk 8). Shadow mission as discipline. Acts 13 calling discernment for one Discerner. And honest assessment of where the cohort actually landed.

**Whole-room versus cohort-split logic**

Six weeks are whole-room (1, 4, 7, 8, 10, 12) and six are cohort-split (2, 3, 5, 6, 9, 11). The pattern is not arbitrary. Whole-room weeks engage the cohort as a body — onboarding (1), corporate mechanism (4), corporate listening (7, 8), Acts 13 commissioning (10), sending (12). Cohort-split weeks engage individual diagnostic and standing-pair work — the soils and knots (2, 3), confession (5), individual hearing (6), shadow naming (9), and the cohort’s self-assessment (11).

In v1.1 the operational vocabulary updates accordingly. ‘Lead Companion’ holds the whole-room geometry; ‘cohort Companions’ hold the three cohort spaces (junior, senior, parent); ‘co-Companions’ are the developing Companions on the team who carry specific operational duties (door, name tags, materials, time-keeping) while learning the architecture from inside.

**Section 3 — Container and Co-Processing**

**The four container conditions**

From Vol 2 Exp 8. The four container conditions — Safe, Present, Clear, Intentional — are the practical implementation of Vol 1’s hearing channel law in a group context. Each condition removes a specific class of interference.

## Safe, Present, Clear, Intentional

**SAFE removes the fear of exposure that keeps people performing for approval.**

**PRESENT removes the distraction of past and future that prevents genuine attention to the Spirit’s movement.**

**CLEAR removes the degrading effect of unacknowledged judgment that turns discernment into performance.**

**INTENTIONAL opens the obedience channel in advance — the cohort chooses to obey before knowing the details of what the Spirit will say.**

*When all four are genuinely in place, the cohort’s collective hearing capacity is significantly greater than the sum of individual capacities.*

**Co-processing made visible**

Co-processing is the under-recognized mechanism of group formation. From Vol 2: when one cohort member does specific work in the centre of the room (a confession in Wk 5, a Bringer’s question in Wk 7, a Discerner’s calling in Wk 10), the whole cohort does parallel work even though only one member’s work is visible. The participant who said little this Tuesday may be doing more interior work than the participant who said much.

Going Deeper makes co-processing visible. Wk 4 is the explicit teaching of the mechanism. From Wk 4 forward, the team frames each whole-room session in advance: ‘One member’s work tonight will be visible; the rest of you will be doing the same work parallel; we will surface what surfaced for you in your between-session journaling and pair check-ins.’

**Opening container protocol — every Tuesday**

The opening sequence holds across all twelve weeks:

**1.** Door, name tags, phone-box. Co-Companion (typically Teen cohort Co-Companion) holds this from T-15 min.

**2.** At T-0, doors close. Lead Companion opens with the Aaronic blessing.

**3.** 60-second silent settling. ‘Holy Spirit, you are welcome to do whatever you want to do here tonight. We are submitted.’

**4.** One-word landing from the prior Tuesday — each member, briefly, names where they are coming in from.

**5.** Frame for tonight. Lead Companion names what tonight is and is not, what container holds, what the architecture asks of the cohort.

**Closing container protocol — every Tuesday**

Same three-layer pattern across all twelve weeks:

**1.** Surface what landed. Brief; voluntary; one or two contributions or a one-word around-the-circle, depending on the week.

**2.** Reaffirm the container. ‘What was named here stays in this room.’

**3.** Aaronic blessing. Hands raised if welcomed; cohort speaks it together by Wk 12.

**Parallel circles vs. shared circles**

Cohort-split weeks (2, 3, 5, 6, 9, 11) use parallel circles — three cohorts (junior teens, senior teens, parents) work in parallel in three rooms with three cohort Companions. Whole-room weeks (1, 4, 7, 8, 10, 12) use the shared circle — the cohort sits as one circle of 20–32 chairs with the lead Companion holding the geometry.

The transitions between modes matter. The cohort-split weeks open and close in the shared circle (Block 1, Block 7–8 of each session); the parallel circles run only for the central blocks. The whole-room weeks never split.

**Section 4 — The Four Deepenings**

**Self deepened (Wks 2–3)**

Wk 2 takes the four soils as a working diagnostic, not metaphor. Each participant maps where in their interior each soil is right now — not which soil they ‘are’, but where the four conditions of the soil are operating in different domains of their life.

Wk 3 introduces the four knot types from Vol 2 Exp 2 (grief, shame, fear, anger) and the lie at the root from Vol 2 Exp 3. Each participant names at least one specific knot operating in their interior right now and identifies at least one lie-at-the-root that the knot is wrapped around. The teaching is that no tool releases a knot apart from Spirit-delivered truth, and each knot type has its characteristic entry point.

Standing pairs are assigned at the close of Wk 3. From Wk 4 forward, each participant has one standing pair partner with whom they walk the rest of Going Deeper.

**Others deepened (Wks 4–5)**

Wk 4 makes co-processing visible — the corporate mechanism by which a group session produces fruit across the whole cohort, not just in the participant whose work was on the agenda. This is essential teaching for Going Deeper because the rest of Going Deeper sessions rely on co-processing to do most of their formation work.

Wk 5 returns to confession-and-restoration with the standing pair. James 5:16 in dyadic privacy, with the architecture made explicit: this is what the practice does, this is what it does not do, and this is how to use it across a year. The frame is confession-as-architecture, not confession-as-disclosure. ONE specific pattern, owned without justification, released; the partner receives, prays, speaks restoration.

**God deepened (Wks 6–8)**

Wk 6 extends PROAPT to longer passages and adds the Tell step in the room. The Tell step is the most-skipped PROAPT step and the one that produces the most long-term hearing development.

Wk 7 introduces corporate listening prayer for one Bringer. The Bringer is selected via the worksheet in Section 8 of this handbook (originally Section E of the Addenda). The cohort listens together for 20 minutes for the Bringer’s real present question, then speaks back briefly what each heard. The Bringer receives, weighs, and names what landed.

Wk 8 attempts Group Level 4 work — the cohort listening for what the Spirit is saying about the cohort itself. The Revelation 2–3 frame structures the listening; the cohort weighs whether what was heard rings true; honest data either way.

**Mission deepened (Wks 9–10)**

Wk 9 names gifts and shadow. Each participant names their primary gift (drawing on the four-by-four reference of teacher / shepherd / prophet / servant-admin) and the shadow direction the gift takes when used wrongly or under stress. The standing pair carries the conversation with the witness sentence at the close.

Wk 10 is Acts 13 calling discernment for one Discerner. The Discerner is selected via the worksheet in Section 8 of this handbook (also originally Section E of the Addenda; same five readiness signals; either Path A — cohort-Companion-identified — or Path B — self-nomination). The cohort fasts lunch, listens 15 minutes, surfaces 18 minutes, prays, lays hands if welcomed, and sends.

**Integration and Sending (Wks 11–12)**

Wk 11 honestly assesses where the cohort actually landed across Going Deeper — cohort-split, with each cohort producing a flip-chart picture, then merging for cross-cohort integration. The five-level taxonomy is the working frame; the question is the steady state, not the peak.

Wk 12 closes Going Deeper. Each member shares the question they carry into the Going Out series; standing pairs speak personal blessing over each other; cohort Companions distribute pre-written sending cards with personal blessings; the bridge to the Going Out series is named with specific dates; the cohort speaks the Aaronic over the cohort. Honest non-continuers are sent with the same dignity as Going Out continuers.

**Section 5 — The Companion Team — Formation Companions**

*This section is fully synced with the Formation Companion role described in IJH Volume 5. v1.1 brings the operational team vocabulary into alignment with the broader synthesis role. What the team has been doing all along is named here in its proper category. (See the standalone Vol 5 Vocabulary Insert artifact — IJH\_Vol5\_Vocabulary\_Insert\_for\_Master\_Handbook.docx — for the canonical Companion level definitions, the four modes, and the level-to-role mapping at a glance.)*

**What a Formation Companion is**

From IJH Vol 5: a Formation Companion is a fellow pilgrim on their own journey, willing to help another pilgrim. The Companion is not an expert or authority over the other person’s formation and discipleship. They are not a paid professional therapeutic counselor, psychologist, or psychiatrist. They are a believer further along in their relationship with God, with faith and competence in specific tools that can open another’s heart to more of God.

The word ‘companion’ is load-bearing. It resonates with the partnering developed through Spiritual Direction and Stephen Ministry traditions, where a companion is defined by presence-with rather than expertise-over. It aligns with the pilgrim vocabulary from the Band of Brothers tradition. It embraces the core insight that formation is a journey two people can walk together, one further along on a particular stretch, both still on the road.

**This is not therapy**

## The boundary the Companion must hold

**The Formation Companion is not a licensed professional counselor or therapist. The relationship is not clinical, meaning results-oriented therapy. Instead, the relationship and interaction are process-oriented. Effects of the companioning may be therapeutic; healing and real change do happen, and often. But the architecture is process, not result.**

**In practical terms, a Companion introduces themselves as a trained fellow pilgrim, not as a counselor. They do not diagnose. They do not bill. They do not take clinical notes. They listen. They work within the boundaries of their specific training and refer out when the situation crosses those boundaries. They are transparent with the pilgrim about what they are and what they are not.**

*Going Deeper assumes this boundary is held by every team member. Section 6 (Crisis Protocols) and Appendix D (Referral List) cover when and how to refer.*

**The four contributing traditions**

From Vol 5: four established Christian care traditions — Spiritual Direction, Transformation Prayer Ministry (TPM), Stephen Ministry, and group facilitation in the Band of Brothers and Fellowship of the Heart contexts — have developed mostly independently of one another for decades or centuries. When laid side by side, they converge on a substantial common core. That core centres on the essence of the Formation Companion role; the mode specializations are what make the role operational in a given moment.

## The four traditions in brief

**SPIRITUAL DIRECTION — the oldest tradition; core posture is attending. The director and pilgrim listen together for what God is already doing.**

**TRANSFORMATION PRAYER MINISTRY — the most protocol-specific. A trained Companion helps a person identify emotional pain tied to a specific memory, locate the lie embedded there, and pray for Jesus to reveal his truth.**

**STEPHEN MINISTRY — lay care training; the most accessible certification pathway; emphasizes the ministry of presence and practical care during specific seasons of life.**

**GROUP FACILITATION — from Band of Brothers and Fellowship of the Heart contexts; container-forming, group process, and the discernment of what the moment requires.**

*Going Deeper draws on all four. The Wk 5 confession architecture borrows from Stephen Ministry; the Wk 6 PROAPT and Wk 9 shadow work borrow from spiritual direction and group facilitation; the Wk 7 corporate listening borrows from group process; the Wk 10 Acts 13 commissioning borrows from all four.*

**The three levels of Companion development**

From Vol 5: Companion development is a three-level progression. The levels are cumulative: Companion II includes Companion I, and Companion III includes both. Each level represents substantial growth in all three taxonomy domains (cognitive, affective, psychomotor), not just one. The levels are named so that no level sounds like a permanent station; a Companion at Level I is not lesser, but at the beginning of a long journey.

## The three levels

**COMPANION I — PROTOCOL COMPETENT. Can engage simple, repeatable tools and practices with fidelity. Affective Taxonomy floor: Stage 3 (scripture personally binding). Many Companions remain at Level I for their entire life of service; that is honourable.**

**COMPANION II — MODE PRACTITIONER. Has developed at least one mode specialization at working depth (Spiritual Direction, TPM-style work, Stephen-style care, or deep group process). Affective Taxonomy floor: Stage 4 (values reorganized around scripture). Can lead from behind — helping a pilgrim own their own next steps rather than prescribing.**

**COMPANION III — INTEGRATED PRACTITIONER. Has two or more mode specializations and the capstone competency: real-time discernment of which mode the moment requires, with the capacity to move between modes without losing grounding. Affective Taxonomy floor: Stage 4 or higher, on the journey to Stage 5. Can train and certify other Companions.**

**Integrated vs. closed at each level — the Pharisee pattern in Companion form.** The three levels above name what each level *adds*; they do not name what happens when a Companion has collapsed into a rigid version of the role. The IJH integration reading (Vol 4 §2; the §4e closed-facilitator caveat specifically) makes this explicit: a Companion II or III whose Stage 1 Receiving has died — who is no longer genuinely open to a new word, to a peer challenging them, to a pilgrim's hard question they did not see coming — is not at Level II or III. They have collapsed into the Companion form of the Pharisee. The pattern shows up with role-specific shape at each level: a closed Companion-Receiving becomes Companion-as-gatekeeper (dismissing what does not fit the framework — "I don't need to hear that"); closed Companion-Responding becomes Companion-as-debater (every engagement returns to defending the existing way); closed Companion-Valuing becomes Companion-as-performer (visible Companion-discipline that establishes status with the team or the cohort rather than serving formation); closed Companion-Organization becomes Companion-as-mechanic (running the curriculum where formation should be happening); closed Companion-Characterization becomes the settled rigid Companion-identity whose cohorts no longer surprise anyone, including the Companion. Each is the same pathology — collapse of the dynamic tension between openness and integration — at a different level of the Companion role. The closed Companion reproduces the pattern in the cohorts they companion; a closed Cohort Companion forms a closed cohort, and the closure travels into the next generation through the leaders that cohort sends out. The Companion's own Companion (the supervision relationship described two sub-sections below) is the primary place where the pattern is detected and addressed; this is the practical operational meaning of "every Companion has a Companion," and the strongest argument for why that relationship is non-negotiable.

**A word on our words.** The architecture in this handbook — the levels, the taxonomies, the four Connects, the knot map, the MSFIG group stages — is scaffolding for the Companion, not a language the cohort must learn. No participant is ever assessed on their fluency in it. A teen who can recite the four knot types and name their Affective-Taxonomy stage has learned vocabulary; a teen who brought one honest thing to their pair has been formed. When you teach, translate the insider-tokens back into kitchen-table English — "a knot" is just "a tangled-up place in you that the day keeps catching on" — and if a participant starts performing the dialect back to you, gently set it down and ask what is actually true for them. The words are ours to hold lightly so the person can be held closely.

**And one line for the room.** If holding the architecture is crowding out presence with the person in front of you — if you are running the protocol in your head while a teen is trying to tell you something real — drop the architecture. The framework exists to serve the encounter; the moment it competes with the encounter, the encounter wins. You can always pick the structure back up; you cannot get back the moment you spent managing it instead of being there.

**Hearing and obeying — the developmental spine**

From Vol 5: scriptural engagement and hearing-and-obeying God are NOT prerequisites (checkbox items the Companion either has or does not have). They are the developmental spine of the Companion role, and they continue to develop across all three levels of Companion maturity. Operationally, this means the Companion’s own Affective Taxonomy stage is not a one-time checkpoint but a continuously developing variable that governs what the Companion can and cannot do at any given time.

This is why every Going Deeper team meeting begins with the team’s own hearing posture, not with task allocation. The team that has not been listening cannot help the cohort listen.

**The four Connects as the Companion’s own formation**

The Companion is a pilgrim. The four Connects architecture from Vol 2 applies to the Companion’s own life as much as to any pilgrim they serve. The Companion who is not connecting with self cannot help another connect with self. The Companion who is not connecting with God in an ongoing and honest way cannot help another connect with God. The Companion who is isolated from a real community of peers cannot model the Connect-with-Others axis. The Companion who has no mission beyond companioning has stopped becoming.

This is why the team retreat between Wk 6 and Wk 7 is structurally required, not optional. The team enters the second half of Going Deeper having had its own Connect work renewed. The handbook v1.0 named this retreat; v1.1 names it as a Formation Companion practice, not just a logistical meeting.

**Every Companion has a Companion**

## The non-negotiable

**From Vol 5: ‘Every Companion has a Companion. Every director has a director. This is non-negotiable. A Companion who is themselves not being companioned has stopped growing, and a Companion who has stopped growing is heading for a ditch.’**

**Operationally for Going Deeper: each team member has a named Companion outside the team — a spiritual director, a more mature Companion, or a peer-supervision pair with someone equally invested. The named Companion is reviewed annually; if the relationship has lapsed, the team member arranges a replacement before continuing.**

*The Lead Companion’s own Companion is named to the team; team transparency about ongoing formation is part of how the team itself stays honest.*

**Operational roles within Going Deeper**

The Going Deeper architecture uses three operational roles. v1.0 called these Lead Companion / Cohort Companion / Co-Companion. v1.1 calls them Lead Companion / Cohort Companion / Co-Companion. The operational distinctions are the same; the role identity is now named in its proper Vol 5 category.

## The three operational roles

**LEAD COMPANION. Holds the whole-room geometry on whole-room weeks; holds the team’s overall arc across all twelve weeks. Typically Companion II or III. Carries primary responsibility for the architecture, the team’s formation across Going Deeper, and the integration of the pilot data. The Lead Companion’s own Companion is named to the team.**

**COHORT COMPANION. Holds one of the three cohort spaces (junior teens, senior teens, parents) on cohort-split weeks; participates in the shared circle on whole-room weeks. Typically Companion I or II. Knows the cohort members by name, by gift, by shadow, by knot, by standing pair partnership. Carries the bulk of pastoral 1:1 follow-up across Going Deeper.**

**CO-COMPANION. Carries specific operational duties (door, name tags, materials, time-keeping, pastoral / clinical contact list) while learning the architecture from inside. Typically a developing Companion working toward Level I or working within Level I. Pairs with a more senior Companion as their training Companion through Going Deeper.**

*All three are Formation Companions. The operational distinctions reflect the demands of the session architecture, not a hierarchy of value.*

**Level expectations for the Going Deeper team**

From Vol 5’s framework: a body whose lead is at Companion III can hold the most complex sessions (Wks 7, 8, 10) without external supervision. A body whose lead is at Companion II can hold most sessions but should arrange specific supervision or consultation for the highest-stakes sessions. A body whose lead is at Companion I should not yet attempt the full Going Deeper architecture; the cohort and the lead are not yet at the level required.

## Working assumption for the CCA pilot

**Lead Companion: Companion II minimum, Companion III preferred. The Wks 7, 8, 10 architecture asks for mode-discernment in real time.**

**Cohort Companions: Companion I working toward II, or Companion II. The cohort circle work asks for confident protocol-fidelity and the capacity to redirect drift in the moment.**

**Co-Companions: developing Companions or Companion I; learning by being inside the architecture with senior Companions present.**

*If the team’s composition does not meet these floors, the lead Companion arranges supervision or consultation from a Companion III outside the team. Pastoral / clinical backup is separately confirmed for every Tuesday regardless of team level.*

**The junior-room floor (deep-work weeks).** On the weeks that go to depth with the youngest cohort — Going Deeper Wk 3 (Knots), Wk 5 (Confession), Wk 9 (Shadow / Mission), and Getting Started Wk 7 (Garden) and Wk 8 (Doubts) — the floor attaches to the *person in the junior room*, not to the team average. A Companion I must not lead junior depth-work alone. And the pastoral / clinical backup must be reachable *that evening* — not merely “on call” — whenever a junior deep-work week runs.

**The crisis-response steps — carried forward in full**

So this section is *terminal* and no Companion needs a second document in the moment, the crisis-response steps are reproduced here. *(Carried forward verbatim from the Getting Started Handbook Section 6, which remains the canonical origin — if one is updated, update both. The pocket Crisis Quick-Reference Card carries the condensed version for the moment itself.)*

### If a teen discloses suicidal ideation or self-harm

1. In the room: do not panic. Do not make a scene. Do not bring it into the larger circle. Acknowledge: “Thank you for telling me. That took courage. We’re going to talk more about this right after we close.”
2. Immediately after session close: bring the teen to a private space with the Lead Companion and the teen Co-Companion (two adults present, never one alone).
3. Listen. Do not interrogate. Do not try to talk them out of it. Ask: “Are you safe right now? Are you having thoughts of hurting yourself today?” Note: do not name specific methods or details — if the teen begins describing methods, gently redirect.
4. Call the parent. Always. Before the teen leaves the building. If you have any reason to believe the parent’s response will endanger the teen, call CPS first.
5. Hand off to qualified clinical care. The pastoral / clinical backup person is named on the Crisis Quick-Reference Card, confirmed at the team pre-meet, and has been alerted in advance.
6. Document what happened, in writing, the same night. Keep the documentation in CCA’s designated secure location, not in personal notes.
7. If in any doubt about acute risk, call 988 (Suicide and Crisis Lifeline) or 911.

### If a teen discloses abuse

1. In the room: same response — do not make a scene. “Thank you for trusting me with that. Let’s talk more right after.”
2. Do not promise to keep it secret. Do not investigate. Do not confront the alleged abuser. The Companion’s only job is to report.
3. Call CPS hotline (1-800-552-7096) within the timeframe required by Virginia law and CCA policy. Notify CCA leadership simultaneously.
4. Do not call the parent unless explicitly directed by CPS — the alleged abuser may be in the home.
5. Document, document, document.

**Three new risk categories in Going Deeper**

Three categories of risk that Getting Started rarely surfaced will surface more often in Going Deeper:

**1. Knot work that surfaces trauma**

Wks 3, 5, 7, and 9 will surface material that may be trauma rather than ordinary spiritual blockage. The architecture is diagnostic, not therapeutic; if a participant’s knot work reveals what is plausibly clinical trauma, the cohort Companion makes a referral to clinical support outside the session. Appendix D has the categories. The team’s discipline: a knot is named, the Companion does not attempt to release it, the Spirit’s timing and the appropriate supports are honoured.

**2. Marriage and family material**

Standing pairs, confession blocks, and shadow work will sometimes surface marriage or family-of-origin material. The architecture is one-pilgrim-at-a-time; the Companion does not attempt to adjudicate marital or family-system dynamics. Pastoral 1:1 outside the session is the path. If the family is intact in the cohort (a parent and a teen across cohorts; a married couple across cohorts), cross-cohort awareness is held by the team without naming the dynamics in either cohort circle.

**3. Gnostic-community amplification of error**

From Vol 2 Exp 9: a community that closes its feedback loop by treating internal consensus as self-validating becomes a gnostic community and ceases to amplify hearing. The Wks 7, 8, and 10 architectures explicitly require the cohort to weigh contributions; the Lead Companion is alert to any dynamic that resembles cohort group-think. Specific contributions are humble offerings, not prophetic decrees; the body weighs together; coercive contributions are gently corrected in real time.

**Mandatory reporting (Virginia)**

Virginia law on mandatory reporting applies. Reasonable suspicion of abuse, neglect, or exploitation of a minor under 18 triggers reporting to Virginia CPS (1-800-552-7096). Reasonable suspicion of abuse or exploitation of a vulnerable adult triggers reporting to Virginia APS (1-888-832-3858). The team follows the law without negotiation when a disclosure crosses the threshold. The two-adult rule applies to any reporting conversation.

**Pastoral/clinical backup confirmed in advance**

Every Tuesday of Going Deeper has confirmed pastoral and clinical backup. The contact name and number are confirmed at the team pre-meet 48 hours before each Tuesday and are in every Companion’s pocket on the Crisis Quick-Reference Card. Appendix D of this handbook has the five-category referral skeleton; CCA pastoral staff populates the local Warrenton-specific contacts before the pilot begins.

**Two-adult rule**

Any 1:1 with a minor (a teen) is held with the two-adult rule — either two adults present, or the conversation in a public space within sight of another adult. This applies to pastoral 1:1 follow-up between sessions and to any cohort Companion’s individual conversation with a teen cohort member. In the language of the Formation Companion role, this is the *never one-on-one* rule: “1:1” here always means individualized care held under the two-adult rule, never a single adult alone with a teen.

**Section 7 — Onboarding Protocol (Evening 1)**

*v1.1 integrates the Onboarding Evening 1 Story-Share Addition (originally Section D of the Addenda Pack v1.2) into Section 7. The vocabulary-only default of v1.0 is replaced by the architecture below.*

**Why onboarding matters**

Going Deeper Wk 1 assumes the cohort has the four-conditions container (Safe, Present, Clear, Intentional). Getting Started alumni built that container together across fifteen weeks. New participants — friends and family of Getting Started families, teens whose parent is not continuing, parents whose teen is not, anyone joining at Going Deeper — have not been in the room when the container was built. Evening 1 is where new participants enter the container.

Onboarding has two architectural goals. First, vocabulary and architecture: new participants leave Evening 1 knowing the four Connects, the four container conditions, the Aaronic blessing, the cohort split, the standing pair structure, and the twelve-week arc at a glance. Second, story-share: each new participant tells a brief witnessed version of their story, with veteran witness, so they enter Wk 1 having already been heard by some of the room.

**Evening 1 architecture (3 hours total)**

Hour 1 — vocabulary and architecture. Hour 2 — the witnessed story-share (new in v1.1). Hour 3 — four-Connects micro-experience plus Q&A. The team holds Evening 1 the week before Wk 1; same room, same time as Tuesdays.

**Hour 1 — vocabulary and architecture (60 min)**

## Six blocks

**WELCOME (5 min). Lead Companion welcomes new participants by name; introduces the team; names what the next three hours will hold.**

**THE FOUR CONNECTS (15 min). Self / Others / God / Mission. Brief teaching with examples from Getting Started that veterans can confirm.**

**THE FOUR CONTAINER CONDITIONS (10 min). Safe / Present / Clear / Intentional. Why each one matters; what each one removes.**

**THE TWELVE-WEEK ARC (10 min). The Hebrews 12 architecture across Going Deeper. Brief; not exhaustive.**

**THE COHORT SPLIT AND STANDING PAIRS (10 min). How the rooms are organized; when the cohort splits; when it joins; what the standing pair is.**

**Q&A (10 min). New participant questions; veterans contribute; the team answers.**

**Hour 2 — the witnessed story-share (60 min)**

This is the new architecture in v1.1. Each new participant tells a brief 5-minute version of their story to the room of veterans plus other new participants. Veterans have been pre-assigned (one veteran per new participant) to be the designated witness for that participant’s story. The witness-back protocol is taught at the start of the block and modeled by the lead Companion.

## Five elements of the story-share architecture

**ONE — 5 minutes per new participant. Strict. The frame is brevity-with-honesty, not exhaustive autobiography.**

**TWO — Three prompts: Where you came from. What brought you here. What is alive in you right now. The new participant chooses how to weight the three.**

**THREE — The room of Getting Started alumni witnesses in silence. No cross-talk during the story. No nodding to encourage. No interpreting facial expressions.**

**FOUR — After each story, ONE designated veteran speaks back briefly: ‘I heard that you \_\_\_\_\_. Thank you for trusting us with this.’ 30–60 seconds. Not a summary; a witness.**

**FIVE — The Lead Companion names a specific observation across the new participants at the end of the block: ‘What I notice across what we just heard is \_\_\_\_\_.’ One sentence; specific.**

**Designated veteran witnesses**

The Lead Companion pre-assigns ONE veteran to be the witness-back voice for each new participant’s story. The assignments are made before Evening 1; the veterans know who they are responsible for receiving. Distribute the assignments so that every veteran receives at most ONE story; this prevents the same veteran from carrying the weight of multiple witnessings.

Veterans are coached briefly before Evening 1 in the witness-back protocol: ‘When [new participant] finishes their 5 minutes, take a breath. Then say: I heard that you \_\_\_\_\_\_. Name what you actually heard. Specific. Not what you would have said in their place; what they said. Close with a brief acknowledgment of trust: Thank you for telling us. 30 to 60 seconds total. No advice; no story-of-your-own; no comparison.’

**Math and timing**

The block is 60 minutes. Each story-share runs 5 minutes; each witness-back runs 60 seconds; transitions add 30–60 seconds. So each new participant’s cycle is approximately 7 minutes. The block can absorb 7–8 new participants comfortably.

If a pilot has 10 or more new participants, the block runs 75 minutes (with the third-hour blocks compressed accordingly), or the new participants are split across two evenings of onboarding. Most pilots will have 4–7 new participants; the 60-minute block is sufficient.

**Hour 3 — four-Connects micro-experience plus Q&A (60 min)**

## Three blocks

**MICRO-EXPERIENCE (35 min). A brief experiential moment in each of the four Connects. 8 minutes each, with brief landing in between. Self: a 1-minute silent inventory of where you are right now. Others: a 5-minute one-direction-each pair share around ‘what brought you to this room.’ God: a 5-minute PROAPT lite on a brief passage. Mission: a 1-sentence written naming of ‘a place I sense God is asking me to show up.’**

**BETWEEN-EVENING-1-AND-WK-1 PRACTICE (10 min). The Rhythm Card distributed; the brief daily practice for the week named; brief Q&A.**

**AARONIC CLOSE (5 min) and informal time (10 min). Optional water and snacks; lingering as new and veteran participants meet briefly.**

**What the Evening 1 story-share changes downstream**

Wk 1 (Welcome Back) is the interlude share for Getting Started alumni — each veteran briefly names where the interlude between Getting Started and Going Deeper left them. With the Evening 1 story-share, new participants do NOT repeat their full story at Wk 1; they participate in the interlude share with whatever brief share is honest for them at that moment.

Wks 2, 3 (the cohort-split diagnostic weeks) assume the new participants have been heard by at least one veteran (their designated witness) and have basic vocabulary. The cohort Companion holds the new-participant-specific care: brief warm contact between Evening 1 and Wk 1; brief check-in at the start of Wk 1 to make sure the architecture is sticking.

Standing pairs (assigned at Wk 3 close) intentionally pair some new participants with veterans where the energy is right. This is a cohort Companion decision; the Lead Companion confirms; the standing-pair covenant is signed at Wk 3 close per Section 8.

**Section 8 — Pairing and the Cohort**

*v1.1 integrates Sections A, B, and E from the Addenda Pack v1.2 — Tell Buddies, Triad Variants, and Bringer / Discerner Selection — into Section 8 as native architecture, not addenda.*

**The standing pair (assigned at Wk 3 close)**

Each participant has ONE standing pair partner from the close of Wk 3 through the close of Wk 12. The pair walks the Wks 4–12 work together — confession block, shadow conversation, weekly check-ins, the Wk 12 pair blessing. The standing pair is the load-bearing relational unit of Going Deeper after the cohort circle itself.

Pair assignments are made by the cohort Companions in consultation with the Lead Companion at the team pre-meet 48 hours before Wk 3. Assignments draw on what cohort Companions have observed across Wks 1–2: which dyads have the right energy, which dyads need cross-cohort visibility, which dyads should be deliberately matched for diverse experience.

Pairs sign the Standing Pair Covenant at the close of Wk 3 (template in Appendix E). The covenant is renewed at Wk 5 with the confession addendum, and again at Wk 9 with the shadow conversation addendum.

**Section A — Tell Buddies (cross-cohort fallback partners)**

Going Deeper has whole-room Tell merges in Wks 6 (PROAPT extended), 8 (cohort hears itself), and 10 (Acts 13). Most participants find cross-cohort partners naturally during the merge windows; a small subset will not. The Tell Buddies system pre-assigns 2–3 cross-cohort fallback candidates for each at-risk participant so the merge produces no awkward orphans.

**Why this exists**

The participants who struggle to find cross-cohort partners are predictable in advance: a junior teen whose only natural cross-cohort connection is their own parent (whom the family-across-cohorts protocol limits); a senior teen who has not yet built informal relationships with the parent cohort; a parent whose teen is not in the program; a new participant who only has the Evening 1 story-share plus a few weeks of context with the cohort. Without pre-planning, these participants may end up unpaired, ashamed, or paired by emergency triage that feels exposing.

**How the team builds the Tell Buddies list**

**1.** At the team pre-meet 48 hours before Wk 6 (and again before Wk 8 and Wk 10), each cohort Companion identifies participants in their cohort who would struggle to find a cross-cohort partner naturally.

**2.** For each at-risk participant, identify 2–3 candidates from a different cohort who would graciously receive an emergency-pair invitation. Senior teens are often natural Tell Buddies for junior teens; experienced parents are often natural Tell Buddies for new-participant teens or new-participant parents.

**3.** Confirm the Tell Buddy candidates before each whole-room Tell session, with a single sentence: ‘On Tuesday, if a participant has not found a Tell partner by the end of the merge window, would you be willing to be a graceful backup pair? Most likely you will not be activated; we just want a small set of participants who will respond without surprise.’

**4.** Maintain the Tell Buddies list privately. The at-risk participants do not know they are on the list. The Tell Buddies do not know who they are buddied with.

**5.** During the Wk 6 (or 8, or 10) merge, the Lead Companion and one cohort Companion visibly float, watching for unpaired participants. After 3 minutes, the cohort Companion approaches an unpaired participant and a Tell Buddy candidate; gracefully forms the pair; the Tell proceeds.

**Special cases the Tell Buddies system does NOT solve**

## Two cases that need separate handling

**FAMILY-ACROSS-COHORTS. The Tell Buddy is NEVER auto-assigned to be the participant’s own family member. The protocol on family Tells (governed by H6.3 in the Wk 6 session document) governs family Tells: brief, specific, complete in the moment, no follow-on conversation in the room.**

**ACTIVE RELATIONAL RUPTURE. If two participants have an unaddressed conflict (a divorcing couple, a teen-parent conflict at high heat, a friendship rupture), the Tell Buddies list does NOT pair them. The cohort Companions triage these explicitly in the team pre-meet; pastoral 1:1 outside the session is the path.**

**Section B — Triad Variants (for odd-numbered cohorts)**

If a cohort has an odd number of participants (5, 7, 9, 11), one triad will form in the cohort’s standing-pair structure. The triad runs the same architecture as pairs in Wks 3, 5, 6, 9, with the third role rotating between observer and participant.

**How the triad works**

Three roles: Speaker, Listener, Witness. Each pair-equivalent block runs three rotations rather than two. In a 30-minute block, each rotation is 10 minutes — long enough for substantive work; short enough to fit the existing architecture.

## The three rotations

**ROTATION 1: Person A speaks; Person B listens; Person C witnesses (silent, holds posture of attentive presence).**

**ROTATION 2: Person B speaks; Person C listens; Person A witnesses.**

**ROTATION 3: Person C speaks; Person A listens; Person B witnesses.**

**After all three rotations: 60 seconds of silent integration; brief witness sentence from each member to each other member they listened to.**

**Where triads apply**

Wk 3 diagnostic exercise (10 min per direction across three rotations). Wk 5 confession block (12 min per direction; the triad is harder here — some triads may opt to function as a pair-plus-trusted-witness rather than three full rotations). Wk 6 PROAPT extended (10 min per direction). Wk 9 shadow conversation (12 min per direction).

**When NOT to form a triad**

## Three conditions that should be solved differently

**If the cohort has 1 odd participant who is consistently struggling, that participant may instead pair with a cohort Companion as a temporary second pair partnership. The cohort Companion is not a full standing-pair partner; the architecture is supportive, not symmetric.**

**If the cohort has multiple odd participants and the available triads would create awkward dynamics (three teens who are all friends from school; three parents who are all in similar life-stages and would not produce diverse contributions), the Lead Companion makes individual decisions about cross-cohort temporary pairings.**

**If a cohort is so small (4 or fewer) that triads dominate, the architecture itself should be reviewed. A cohort of 4 may run as two pairs cleanly; a cohort of 3 may merge with another cohort for the standing-pair work specifically.**

**Section E — Bringer Selection Worksheet (for Wk 7) and Discerner Selection (for Wk 10)**

**The five readiness signals**

## Used for both Bringer (Wk 7) and Discerner (Wk 10) selection

**ONE — Real present question. Not theoretical, not extracted from the past. A question alive in the participant’s life this season. For Wk 7, any present question of any size; for Wk 10, a specific calling at a specific discernment point.**

**TWO — Honest engagement with the prior weeks’ work. The participant has been in the room, named knots in Wk 3, attended confession in Wk 5, practiced extended PROAPT in Wk 6, named gift and shadow in Wk 9 (for Wk 10 only).**

**THREE — Capacity to hold contributions. The Bringer / Discerner must be able to receive what the cohort says without flooding, dissociation, defensiveness, or grasping for unanimity. Some of what surfaces will be useful; some will not; some will contradict.**

**FOUR — Standing pair functioning. The participant has an active pair partnership; the daily integration across the week is held by the pair plus cohort Companion plus Lead Companion. A pair that is not functioning means the participant lacks load-bearing structure across the integration window.**

**FIVE — (For Wk 10 only.) Discernment supports already in place. The Discerner has, beyond the cohort, at least one of: spouse / parents in the discernment, pastor or spiritual director, a specific person with vocational expertise in the area being discerned. Wk 10 is one input among several.**

**Path A — cohort-Companion-identified**

At the team pre-meet 48 hours before Wk 7 (or two weeks before Wk 10), each cohort Companion brings 1–2 candidates from their cohort who meet the readiness signals. The team triages; the Lead Companion weighs in but does not unilaterally decide; the cohort Companion who knows the candidate best holds primary discernment. ONE Bringer or Discerner is selected; backup candidates are noted for future possible roles.

**Path B — self-nomination**

Beginning at Wk 5 (for Wk 7’s Bringer) or Wk 8 (for Wk 10’s Discerner), the Lead Companion announces to the whole cohort that the upcoming centre-work session will use a volunteer, and that any cohort member with a real present question (Wk 7) or a specific calling at a discernment point (Wk 10) may self-nominate. Self-nomination is by 1-page written pre-frame submitted to the Lead Companion.

Self-nominees are reviewed by the team at the pre-meet against the same five readiness signals. Where multiple self-nominees emerge, the team selects one and offers the others a future possibility (a future Going Out series session, ongoing pastoral 1:1 attention to the question or calling, or a later iteration of Going Deeper if the cohort runs again).

**How the team uses both paths**

Both paths are valid. Most pilots will use one or the other; some will use both in parallel. The team’s critical discipline is honesty about which path is producing genuine readiness. A self-nominee who fails the readiness signals is honoured but not selected; a cohort-Companion-identified candidate who has not consented or is hesitant is not pressured.

**Section 9 — The Twelve Weekly Sessions — Brief Sketches**

*Full lesson plans are in the twelve session documents. The sketches below give the team the architecture at a glance; team members read the full session plan in the week before each Tuesday and pre-meet 48 hours before.*

**Wk 1 — Welcome Back (whole-room)**

Re-formation after the interlude. Veterans share interlude landings; new participants integrate. The Hebrews 12 architecture is named. Standing pairs are foreshadowed (assigned at Wk 3 close). Practices are reset: Rhythm Card, Examen, daily PROAPT continue.

**Wk 2 — The Soils Re-Examined (cohort-split)**

Vol 2’s four soils as working diagnostic. Each participant maps where in their interior each soil is right now, in different domains (work, relationships, prayer, body). Cohort Companions help the participants name specifically. No therapeutic release this week; diagnostic only.

**Wk 3 — Knots and Lies (cohort-split, diagnostic capstone)**

Vol 2’s four knot types (grief, shame, fear, anger) and the lie at the root. Each participant names at least one knot and identifies one lie. Standing pairs are assigned at the close. Standing Pair Covenant is signed.

**Wk 4 — Co-Processing in the Room (whole-room)**

Vol 2’s teaching on co-processing made explicit. ONE volunteer brings a specific knot from Wk 3; the cohort Companion works with them in the centre while the room co-processes silently. After: each silent participant names what surfaced in their own parallel work. The mechanism is exposed; the rest of Going Deeper depends on it.

**Wk 5 — Confession and Restoration, Deepened (cohort-split, standing pairs)**

James 5:16 in standing pairs. ONE specific pattern, owned without justification, released; the partner receives, prays, speaks restoration. 25 minutes per direction. Architecture, not disclosure. The Vol 2 Exp 4 high-confidence law (90% confidence rating) is the basis.

**Wk 6 — PROAPT, Extended (cohort-split)**

PROAPT on a longer passage (Romans 12 or Psalm 32). The Tell step done in the room with cross-cohort partners. The Tell step is the most-skipped PROAPT step and the most consequential for long-term hearing development. Tell Buddies (Section 8) are deployed if needed.

**Wk 7 — Corporate Listening Prayer (whole-room)**

ONE Bringer with a real present question (selected per Section 8). The cohort listens together for 20 minutes; speaks back briefly what each heard FOR the Bringer; the Bringer receives and weighs. 1 Cor 14:29 is the architecture.

**Wk 8 — What the Group Hears About Itself (whole-room)**

Group Level 4 attempt. The cohort listens for what the Spirit may be saying about the cohort itself. Revelation 2–3 frame. 12 minutes silent listening, around-the-circle surfacing of ‘what I heard about US’, lead Companion’s pattern integration, cohort weighing. Two outcomes are equally honest: convergence, or honest data of not-yet-Level-4.

**Wk 9 — The Shadow Mission (cohort-split, standing pairs)**

Gifts and shadow. The four-by-four (teacher / shepherd / prophet / servant-admin and their shadow directions). Matthew 23 as central case. Standing-pair shadow conversation: 18 minutes per direction with the witness sentence. Architecture is observation, not condemnation.

**Wk 10 — Acts 13 Calling Discernment (whole-room)**

ONE Discerner with an emerging calling (selected per Section 8 — either Path A or Path B). Cohort fasts lunch. Discerner frames calling for 12 min; cohort listens 15 min; surfaces 18 min; prays and lays hands if welcomed; sends. Discerner walks 30–90 day integration window with full team support.

**Wk 11 — Where Is Our Cohort (cohort-split, then merge)**

Honest assessment. Each cohort produces a flip-chart picture using the six-question worksheet (the worksheet handles both possible Wk 8 outcomes). The merge integrates three honest pictures. Five-level taxonomy is the working frame; the question is the steady state, not the peak. The Companion team brings its year of weekly [Three Vital Signs](../shared/three-vital-signs.md) readings to the pre-meet — costly telling, response to load, the sought stray — as the witnessed record against which the cohort's self-picture is lovingly checked; where the self-picture and the signs disagree, the signs are senior (a room never self-reports its own silence).

**Wk 12 — Sending and Bridge (whole-room)**

Each member shares the question they carry into the Going Out series. Standing pairs speak personal blessing over each other. Cohort Companions distribute pre-written sending cards. The bridge to the Going Out series is named with specific dates (placeholders until pilot calendar is locked). The cohort speaks the Aaronic over the cohort. Honest non-continuers are sent with the same dignity as Going Out continuers.

**Section 10 — Bridging to the Going Out series**

**The shape of the bridge**

The Going Out series is the third series of the arc. Twelve weeks. A cohort that landed at Level 3 with Level 4 ahead enters Going Out at Level 3; Going Out will not pretend otherwise.

**Appendix B — The Knot Map (Vol 2 Exp 2: Emotional Knots)**

*The four kinds of emotional knot the cohort engages in Wk 3 and continues to walk across the rest of Going Deeper. Each knot has a characteristic entry point; no tool releases a knot apart from Spirit-delivered truth.*

## The four knot types

**GRIEF — the knot of loss not fully mourned. Entry point: the specific loss is named, with what was actually lost (not generic). The Spirit delivers presence; the pilgrim is not alone in the loss.**

**SHAME — the knot of self-as-defective. Entry point: the specific lie about self is named (‘I am \_\_\_\_\_’). The Spirit delivers identity-truth; the pilgrim hears who they are in Christ.**

**FEAR — the knot of anticipated harm. Entry point: the specific fear is named (‘I am afraid that \_\_\_\_\_’). The Spirit delivers presence-with-protection; ‘do not fear, for I am with you’.**

**ANGER — the knot of injustice not yet released to God. Entry point: the specific injustice is named, with what was actually taken or violated. The Spirit delivers vindication-trust; the pilgrim releases to the Judge of all the earth.**

**Diagnostic, not therapeutic**

Going Deeper Wk 3 names the knot. The release is the Spirit’s work; the cohort circle is not the therapeutic venue; the cohort Companion does not attempt knot release in the cohort space. **Appendix C — The Five-Level Group Taxonomy (MSFIG — IJH Vol 5 Formation Document)**

A note on the three Level taxonomies in the IJH corpus.

Three different five-level frameworks operate in the FotH curriculum, and they share the surface vocabulary “Level 1 … Level 5.” On first reading they can be confusing. Each is theologically distinct and addresses a different unit of formation.

Affective Taxonomy (educational psychology; Vol 2 Exp 7). Tracks an individual participant’s maturity of engagement: Receiving → Responding → Valuing → Organization → Characterization. Used in Getting Started to set per-cohort outcomes and across Going Deeper to read individual development.

Group Level taxonomy (MSFIG; cf. Vol 2 Exp 8 + Exp 9) — this appendix. Tracks a cohort’s culture as a body: Level 1 co-presence → Level 2 information-sharing → Level 3 real work → Level 4 body that hears together → Level 5 apostolic team sent together. Used in GD Wk 11 (honest cohort assessment) and Going Out Wk 11.

Leader Level taxonomy (Going Out Handbook Section 4). Tracks a facilitator’s development across the years following first being sent to lead: from Receiving the Call through to multi-generational, self-replicating leadership. Used only in Going Out.

When a session document says “Level 3,” it is naming one of these three. In ambiguous moments, the team writes out which: “the cohort is at Group Level 3” or “the leader is at Leader Level 2.” Affective Taxonomy levels are usually named in full because the section header sets the context.

**The integration reading applies across all three taxonomies.** None of the three is read as stage replacement under the IJH integration framing (V2.Exp7 pastoral home; Vol 4 §2 construct-level home). A participant at Affective Taxonomy Level 4 who has lost Level 1 Receiving is not at Level 4; they are rigid. A Group Level 4 cohort that has lost the willingness to receive a new voice is not at Group Level 4; it is closed. A Leader Level 4 leader who runs the curriculum where formation should be happening is not at Leader Level 4; they are a curriculum-runner wearing a Level 4 label. The Pharisee-collapse pattern is the same shape at every level of every taxonomy: the dynamic tension between openness and integration has collapsed to one pole. The two FotH places that work this out for the team in role-specific Pharisee vocabulary are the "Integrated vs. closed at each level" paragraph in the GD handbook's "three levels of Companion development" section and the closing "Integration check across the levels — the Leader-Pharisee pattern" sub-section in the Going Out Inviting Others Handbook Section 4. Read those for the operational diagnostics at the Companion and Leader registers; the Group-cohort register's analog is the §4b GATS closure shadow rating in IJH Vol 4 and the Group SIP closure-flag rule that operationalizes it.

## The five levels

**LEVEL 1 — People in the same room. Co-presence without acknowledgment.**

**LEVEL 2 — People who know each other and share information. Most small groups operate here.**

**LEVEL 3 — People who do real work together. The cohort circle level. Specific work surfaces in specific members; the room holds it. Wks 2–7 of Going Deeper operate here.**

**LEVEL 4 — A body that hears together. The group has agency; the Spirit speaks to it as a unit. Wk 8’s attempt.**

**LEVEL 5 — An apostolic team that is sent together. Acts 13:1–3. Wk 10’s attempt for one Discerner.**

**Steady state vs. peak**

Most cohorts have a peak moment that is one level above their steady state. Wk 5 confession or Wk 10 commissioning may produce a moment of higher capacity. The peak is real; it is also not the steady state. The steady state is where the cohort can operate FROM in ordinary conditions.

Wk 11’s assessment is of the steady state. Naming the peak honestly is part of the assessment; pretending the peak is the steady state is the danger.

**Why Level 4 is rare**

Level 4 requires Level 3 first. A body cannot hear together if it is not yet doing real work together. Going Deeper builds Level 3 across Wks 2–7; Wk 8 is the first Level 4 attempt. Level 4 also requires honest data — bodies that perform unity rather than living it cannot weigh what they heard. Level 4 is not permanent; even bodies that reach Level 4 do not stay there constantly.

The cohort’s Wk 11 assessment may honestly be that the cohort attempted Level 4 at Wk 8 and is at Level 3 with Level 4 ahead. That is honest data, not failure. Going Out builds from where the cohort actually is.

**Section A — Tell Buddies**

*To slot into Going Deeper Handbook Section 8 (Pairing and the Cohort), as a sub-section after the Standing Pair Covenant material.*

**Why this exists**

Wk 6 introduced the cross-cohort Tell merge — each participant Tells one specific person from a different cohort what they heard during cohort PROAPT. The merge depends on participants finding cross-cohort partners naturally in a 3-minute window. Most participants will find a partner without help; a small subset will not.

The participants who struggle are predictable in advance: a junior teen whose only natural cross-cohort connection is their own parent (whom the family-across-cohorts protocol limits to brief specific Tells); a senior teen who has not yet built informal relationships with the parent cohort; a parent whose teen is not in the program; a new participant who only has two evenings of context with the cohort. Without pre-planning, these participants may end up unpaired, ashamed, or paired by emergency Companion triage that feels exposing rather than helpful.

Tell Buddies are pre-assigned cross-cohort fallback partners. Each at-risk participant has 2–3 Tell Buddy candidates identified before the session. The Tell Buddies do not know they are designated; the Buddy is only activated if the participant is unpaired after 3 minutes of merge time. The activation is graceful: ‘Hey, [Buddy], can [participant] partner with you for the Tell?’ The Buddy says yes (they have agreed in advance to be available); the pair forms; the Tell happens.

Tell Buddies also apply to Wk 8’s cohort-level hearing share (where some participants may need a partner to walk what they heard), and to Wk 10’s Acts 13 calling discernment (where the merge geometry resembles Wk 6’s).

**How the team builds the Tell Buddies list**

**1.** At the team pre-meet 48 hours before Wk 6, each Cohort Companion identifies participants in their cohort who would struggle to find a cross-cohort partner naturally. Three classes: junior teens whose only cross-cohort family is their own parent; senior teens with no informal parent-cohort relationships; new participants who completed onboarding but have less informal contact with the rest of the cohort.

**2.** For each at-risk participant, identify 2–3 candidates from a different cohort who would graciously receive an emergency-pair invitation. Senior teens are often natural Tell Buddies for junior teens (cross-age but same teen sensibility); experienced parents are often natural Tell Buddies for new-participant teens or new-participant parents.

**3.** Confirm the Tell Buddy candidates before Wk 6, with a single sentence: ‘On Tuesday, if a participant has not found a Tell partner by the end of the merge window, would you be willing to be a graceful backup pair? Most likely you will not be activated; we just want a small set of participants who will respond without surprise.’ Most candidates say yes.

**4.** Maintain the Tell Buddies list privately. The at-risk participants do not know they are on the list. The Tell Buddies do not know who they are buddied with.

**5.** During the Wk 6 merge, the Lead Companion and one Cohort Companion visibly float, watching for unpaired participants. After 3 minutes, the Cohort Companion approaches an unpaired participant and a Tell Buddy candidate; gracefully forms the pair; the Tell proceeds.

**Section B — Triad Variants**

*To slot into Going Deeper Handbook Section 8 (Pairing and the Cohort), as a sub-section after the Tell Buddies material.*

**Why this exists**

The Standing Pair Covenant from Wk 3 assumes pair geometry: two people who walk the rest of Going Deeper together. When a cohort has an odd number of participants — say 7 junior teens, 9 senior teens, 11 parents — the team cannot form pairs without leaving someone out or pulling someone from another cohort (which violates the within-cohort matching principle). The triad variant solves this for any cohort with an odd number.

The triad is one group of three within an otherwise paired cohort. The three walk the rest of Going Deeper together as a triad partnership; the witness dynamic is different from the pair (more witnessing, less depth-per-direction); the architecture in each subsequent week adjusts accordingly.

**When a triad is formed**

At the close of Wk 3, when standing pairs are assigned. Cohort Companions have already considered the cohort’s composition (per Section 8’s pairing principles); if the count is odd, ONE triad is formed. Not two triads, not a triad-plus-pair-in-pair geometry. ONE triad of three.

Choose the triad members carefully. Three people who can hold three voices without the loudest voice dominating. Three people who can sit with two listeners watching their work without performing for either. If one member of a potential triad would be inhibited by being one of three rather than half of two, place that member in a pair and triad three other members.

**Per-session adjustments**

**Wk 3 — Diagnostic exercise (pair-week pairing)**

## The Wk 3 diagnostic pairs are this-week-only and run at 15 minutes per direction. For an odd cohort, the triad geometry runs three rounds of 10 minutes each (one person works, two listen). Cohort Companion floats more attentively; the witness load distributes across two listeners and is therefore less concentrated.

**After Wk 3, the same triad continues as the standing-triad partnership for the rest of Going Deeper, separate from this-week pairing.**

**Wk 4 — Co-Processing (whole-room)**

## No triad-specific adjustment. Wk 4 is whole-room with two volunteer pieces of work; the pair / triad architecture is dormant for the night. The triad members sit together in the circle if they wish; they participate in surfacing rounds individually like everyone else.

**Wk 5 — Confession and Restoration**

## The standard pair architecture is 25 minutes per direction. For the triad: three rounds of 15 minutes each. ONE person confesses; TWO listen, of which one prays specifically and one speaks the blessing of restoration. Roles rotate across the three rounds so each member confesses once, prays once, and blesses once.

**The triad version of confession requires more pre-briefing. The Cohort Companion briefs the triad before the cohort circle splits: ‘Three rounds of 15 minutes. Decide order before you begin. Roles rotate; each of you will confess, pray, and bless. The architecture is the same; the geometry is one-and-two rather than one-and-one.’**

**Confidentiality is more complex in a triad. The H5.2 protocol applies, signed by all three. What is named in the triad stays in the triad, with the standard exceptions.**

**Watch for: in the triad, the confessor may feel doubly-witnessed (more exposed) or less-witnessed (each listener slightly less attentive than a pair partner would be). Cohort Companion floats; checks in offline within the week with the triad members.**

**Wk 6 — PROAPT, Extended**

## Cohort PROAPT is silent and individual; no pair architecture during the silent block. The cohort share at the end of the silent block runs around the circle including all triad members.

**The cross-cohort Tell merge: one triad member partners with the rest of the merge as usual (any cross-cohort partner). The other two triad members EITHER pair with each other for the Tell (within-cohort, exception to the cross-cohort default — acceptable for triads only) OR find separate cross-cohort partners. The Cohort Companion briefs the triad before the merge: ‘You can either tell each other (within your triad) or find separate cross-cohort partners. The within-triad Tell is fine; the cross-cohort Tell is the architecture but flexible for you.’**

**Wk 9 — Shadow Mission**

## Pair work for the gift-and-shadow exercise is 12 minutes per direction. For the triad: three rounds of 8 minutes each. ONE names their gift and its shadow direction; TWO listen and reflect back. Same role-rotation pattern as Wk 5’s confession.

**Watch for: in a triad shadow exercise, the doubled witness can clarify the shadow more than a pair (two perspectives on the same shadow direction); it can also feel more exposing. Cohort Companion briefs both possibilities at the start.**

**Re-pairing rules for triads**

## Three rules

**ONE — the triad is not stronger than a pair. If the triad is not holding by Wk 5, the Cohort Companion can re-pair quietly: form one pair and one pair, with the third person joining a different cohort’s triad if available, or paired with a willing cross-cohort partner with the team’s blessing. The within-cohort default is preferred but not absolute when the triad has clearly failed.**

**TWO — the triad does not get re-paired more than once. If a Cohort Companion re-pairs a failing triad at Wk 5 and the new geometry still does not hold by Wk 7, the conversation becomes pastoral: with the participants, the team, and the Lead Companion together. The participants’ ongoing engagement with Going Deeper is not at stake; the pair partnership specifically is.**

**THREE — if a triad clearly works (as some do, particularly when the three members balance well across age or gender), the team affirms it and runs it through Wk 12 without alteration. Triads that work produce a slightly different formation than pairs — more witnessing across the partnership; less depth per pairwise interaction — and that difference is fine.**

**Section D — Onboarding Evening 1: Story-Share Addition**

*To slot into Going Deeper Handbook Section 7 (Onboarding Protocol), replacing the prior Evening 1 ‘vocabulary-only’ default with a vocabulary-plus-witnessed-story-share architecture.*

**Why this changed**

The original onboarding draft ran two evenings two weeks before Going Deeper Wk 1. Evening 1 was vocabulary (the four Connects briefly, the four conditions, PROAPT taught and practiced, the Garden lightly, the Rhythm Card). Evening 2 was the working depth. The story-share by each new participant was originally placed in Evening 2.

The pilot review identified the order as suboptimal. New participants arriving at Wk 1 with vocabulary but without having been witnessed in story will speak guardedly during the interlude share; veterans will speak more fully because they have eight Tuesdays of being witnessed in Getting Started. The asymmetry compounds across early weeks.

Moving the story-share to Evening 1 second hour evens the asymmetry. By Wk 1, every new participant has been witnessed once by some of the cohort; the interlude share runs more honestly; the Wk 2–3 diagnostic block lands on a foundation of being-known rather than being-vocabularized.

**Revised Evening 1 architecture (3 hours, 7:00–10:00 PM)**

The first hour is the existing vocabulary content; the second hour is the new story-share addition; the third hour is the practice session (PROAPT taught and run) plus close.

| **Time** | **Block** | **Notes** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 6:45–7:00 | Arrival window | Door, name tags, phone-box. Each new participant is greeted by a Getting Started alumnus (the assigned onboarding anchor). |
| 7:00–7:08 | Block 1: Welcome and centering | Aaronic blessing. Frame: vocabulary, then story, then a first taste of the practice. |
| 7:08–7:50 | Block 2: Vocabulary teaching | Four Connects (15 min). Four conditions (10 min). The five-level group taxonomy briefly (5 min). The Personal Heart Journal and Rhythm Card introduced (12 min). |

**Block 3 in detail — the story-share, witnessed**

**Architecture**

## Five elements

**ONE — 5 minutes per new participant. Strict. The frame is brevity-with-honesty, not exhaustive autobiography.**

**TWO — Three prompts: Where you came from. What brought you here. What is alive in you right now. The new participant chooses how to weight the three.**

**THREE — The room of Getting Started alumni witnesses in silence. No cross-talk during the story. No nodding to encourage. No interpreting facial expressions.**

**FOUR — After each story, ONE designated veteran speaks back briefly: ‘I heard that you \_\_\_\_\_. Thank you for trusting us with this.’ 30–60 seconds. Not a summary; a witness.**

**FIVE — The Lead Companion names a specific observation across the new participants at the end of the block: ‘What I notice across what we just heard is \_\_\_\_\_.’ One sentence; specific.**

**Designated veteran witnesses**

The Lead Companion pre-assigns ONE veteran to be the witness-back voice for each new participant’s story. The assignments are made before Evening 1; the veterans know who they are responsible for receiving. Distribute the assignments so that every veteran receives at most ONE story; this prevents the same veteran from carrying the weight of multiple witnessings.

Veterans are coached briefly before Evening 1 in the witness-back protocol: ‘When [new participant] finishes their 5 minutes, take a breath. Then say: ‘I heard that you \_\_\_\_\_\_.’ Name what you actually heard. Specific. Not what you would have said in their place; what they said. Close with a brief acknowledgment of trust: ‘Thank you for telling us.’ 30 to 60 seconds total. No advice; no story-of-your-own; no comparison.’

**Math and timing**

The block is 60 minutes. Each story-share runs 5 minutes; each witness-back runs 60 seconds; transitions add 30–60 seconds. So each new participant’s cycle is approximately 7 minutes. The block can absorb 7–8 new participants comfortably.

If a pilot has 10 or more new participants, the block runs 75 minutes (with the third-hour blocks compressed accordingly), or the new participants are split across two evenings of onboarding. Most pilots will have 4–7 new participants; the 60-minute block is sufficient.

**What this changes downstream**

**Wk 1 (Welcome Back) interlude share**

## The Wk 1 voluntary interlude share runs more honestly. New participants arrive having been witnessed once by some of the room; they are not strangers being asked to share into a familiar group. The asymmetry between veterans and new participants compresses.

**Watch for: a new participant whose Evening 1 story is referenced obliquely during the interlude share. ‘I told you a couple weeks ago that \_\_\_\_\_\_; this week \_\_\_\_\_\_.’ Honor; the room receives.**

**Wk 1 onboarding-anchor seating**

The onboarding anchor for each new participant is, by default, the veteran who served as the witness-back voice during their Evening 1 story-share. The relational continuity strengthens both Wk 1 seating and the new participant’s sense of being known by a specific person, not just being among a group.

**Wks 2–3 cohort circle dynamics**

New participants in cohort circles for the diagnostic and knot work will lean into specificity faster when they have already been heard in story. The Cohort Companions may notice this difference in the first cohort circle of Wk 2; affirm the pattern; the formation is already in motion.

## If Evening 1 cannot be lengthened to 3 hours

*Some pilots will have venue or family-schedule constraints that prevent a 3-hour Evening 1. Two acceptable adjustments:*

**OPTION A — Compress vocabulary teaching to 35 min, run story-share for 60 min, run PROAPT briefly (15 min) and skip the Rhythm Card practice (introduce by handout only). Total: 2 hours 30 min. The Rhythm Card practice migrates to Evening 2 first hour.**

**OPTION B — Keep Evening 1 vocabulary-only at 2 hours; add Evening 1.5 between the two evenings purely for the story-share (90 minutes). This adds an evening but produces the most-witnessed result.**

*Both options preserve the principle: every new participant is witnessed in story before Wk 1 of the cohort.*

**Section E — Bringer Selection Worksheet**

*To slot into Going Deeper Handbook Section 8 (Pairing and the Cohort), as a sub-section after the Tell Buddies material. Used at the team pre-meet 48 hours before Wk 7.*

**Why this exists**

Wk 7 (Corporate Listening Prayer) requires ONE pre-briefed cohort member to bring a real present question into the centre of the room. The cohort prays and listens together for 20 minutes; members speak back what they heard for the Bringer; the Bringer receives, weighs, and integrates across the following week. This is the second-highest-stakes session of Going Deeper after Wk 5, and the spiritual risks (manipulation, false prophecy, cohort coercion) are high enough that the Bringer must be selected with care — not chosen impulsively at the team pre-meet, not auto-selected by who volunteers first.

In the pilot prep cycle, the question came up: should the team identify the Bringer (top-down, based on observed readiness signals from Wks 4–6), or should participants self-nominate (bottom-up, by application starting Wk 5 with team triage at the Wk 6 pre-meet)? The honest answer is that both modes have value at different points in the pilot. This worksheet supports the team-identification mode, which is the default for first-pilot cohorts. The self-nomination mode is named in Wk 10’s logistics where the Acts 13 calling discernment volunteer is at higher stakes and self-nomination is appropriate.

**Readiness signals — what the Cohort Companions look for**

## Five signals across Wks 4–6

**ONE — The participant has a real present question, not theoretical. ‘Should I take this job’ vs ‘what is my calling in life’ — the first is right-sized for Wk 7; the second is right-sized for Wk 10. Bringer questions are typically discernment between two or three concrete options, or a discernment about a specific present situation.**

**TWO — The participant has been honest in Wks 4–6. They surfaced material in the Wk 4 co-processing, named a specific knot in Wk 3, did the architecture in Wk 5’s confession, brought specific Apply-and-Tell content in Wk 6. The Bringer is not someone who has been performing safety in the cohort.**

**THREE — The participant can hold what the room speaks back. Some questions surface in stable participants who can weigh contributions across a week; some questions are connected to crisis material that requires pastoral support, not corporate prayer. The Cohort Companion’s discernment of where the participant currently is matters here.**

**FOUR — The participant’s standing pair has been working. The Bringer goes into the centre of the cohort with their pair partner as a primary support across the integration week; if the pair is fragile, the Bringer is not the right choice this round.**

**FIVE — The participant is willing to be pre-briefed and to bring the question publicly. Bringers must consent specifically, not be drafted. The conversation between the Cohort Companion and the prospective Bringer — ideally on the Saturday before Wk 7 — confirms consent and right-sizes the question.**

**How the team uses this worksheet**

**1.** Each Cohort Companion brings one or two prospective Bringers from their cohort to the team pre-meet 48 hours before Wk 7. Six prospects across three cohorts is the maximum; two prospects per cohort is typical.

**2.** The team reviews each prospect against the five signals. Discusses fit; discusses risks. The Lead Companion weighs in but does not unilaterally decide; the Cohort Companion who knows the prospect best holds primary discernment.

**3.** The team selects ONE Bringer for Wk 7. Only one. The other prospects are noted for future possible roles — Wk 10 calling discernment, future Going Out series, or simply for the Cohort Companion’s ongoing pastoral attention.

**4.** The Bringer’s Cohort Companion schedules a 1:1 with the Bringer for the Saturday before Wk 7. The conversation: ‘We are inviting you to bring a real present question into the centre of the cohort on Tuesday. Here is what that involves; here is what we will protect; here is what we ask of you. You can decline; you can accept and choose the question with us; you can accept tentatively and confirm by Monday evening.’ Most invited Bringers accept; some decline; both are honest.

**5.** If the invited Bringer declines, the team’s second-prospect list is engaged. If no acceptable Bringer can be confirmed by Monday evening, the team has two options: (a) postpone Wk 7’s corporate listening exercise by one week and use Wk 7 as a teaching session about Bringers without an actual Bringer; (b) the Lead Companion brings the question, modeling the architecture as a practitioner. Option (a) is preferred for first-pilot cohorts; option (b) is for Companion teams with significant prior practice.

**Specifying the question with the Bringer**

After the team selects the Bringer, the Cohort Companion works with the Bringer in 1:1 to right-size the question. The architecture for the Bringer’s question is:

## ONE — Real and present, not theoretical or speculative. A discernment between two or three specific options; a question about a specific present situation; a sense the Bringer is trying to test.

**TWO — Right-sized for 8–10 minutes of pre-frame plus 20 min of cohort listening. Not ‘what is my calling’ (that is Wk 10); not ‘what should I do about everything’ (that is therapy).**

**THREE — Not a question that requires private detail the cohort cannot hold. ‘Should I leave my marriage’ surfaces material the cohort cannot adjudicate; ‘how is the Spirit speaking to me about my marriage in this season’ may be appropriate.**

**FOUR — Held by the Bringer, weighed by the Bringer, with the Bringer in charge of integration across the week after. The cohort’s contributions are humble offerings; the discernment belongs to the Bringer in the Spirit’s presence.**

**Special cases**

## Three cases the worksheet does not cleanly handle

**A teen Bringer with a parent in another cohort. Possible but complex. The pre-brief includes a conversation with both teen and parent: the parent will hear their teen’s real question in real time; the parent agrees to remain a silent witness during the surfacing round and to not deploy the question as parental leverage at home. Pastoral 1:1 with the family within the week.**

**A parent Bringer with a teen in another cohort. Same protocol in reverse. The teen agrees to remain silent during surfacing; the parent’s question is not used as a parental modeling lesson; pastoral support follows.**

**A Bringer whose question, on closer inspection, exceeds the architecture (current crisis, suicidality, active relational rupture). Decline gently and pastorally. ‘What you are bringing is bigger than the corporate-listening architecture is for; we want to walk this with you in a more held form.’ Pastoral 1:1; clinical referral as appropriate; the Bringer slot moves to second prospect.**

*Within 24 hours, the Lead Companion and the Bringer’s Cohort Companion have a brief check-in with the Bringer. ‘How did Tuesday land for you? What is rising as you weigh? Do you need additional pastoral support across the integration week?’*

*The Bringer’s standing pair carries the daily integration; the Cohort Companion is the secondary support; the Lead Companion is available if the integration produces something larger than expected.*

*Brief team note within 24 hours: did the Bringer selection feel right? Did the architecture protect the Bringer? What signals proved most predictive of fit? Across pilot weeks the worksheet improves.*

# Appendix H — Continuous-Run Architecture and CCA Pilot Calendar

*This appendix names three pieces of operational architecture that the curriculum needed but had not yet articulated explicitly: the architectural duration of the interlude, the rule about cohort membership across cycles, and the CCA Pilot Calendar template. Together these prepare the curriculum for continuous-run deployment beyond the pilot while honoring the pilot’s actual academic-calendar rhythm.*

## H.1 — The interlude as a 6–10 week architectural element

The interlude between Going Deeper Wk 12 and Going Out Wk 1 is 6–10 weeks by design — long enough for the cohort to disperse into ordinary life and surface material the formal sessions could not, short enough that the cohort can re-form into the Going Out series without losing momentum. The duration is architectural, not calendar-derived: it works whether it spans winter holidays, summer break, or any other 6–10-week period.

Pastoral coverage is full across the interlude; standing pairs continue; the Rhythm Card practice continues; the Personal Heart Journal continues. The interlude is structural, not vacation. A pilot or future cycle that compresses the interlude below 6 weeks risks the cohort entering Going Out without the dispersal that makes Going Out’s reopening architecturally honest. A pilot or future cycle that extends the interlude beyond 10 weeks risks the cohort losing the formation arc’s continuity.

When the host community’s calendar produces an interlude shorter or longer than this window, the team adjusts deliberately rather than absorbing the calendar passively. A 12-week interlude across summer break, for example, can be honored by adding an optional mid-interlude check-in (a 60-minute Companion-led gathering, no formal teaching, just relational continuity) at week 6 of the interlude. A 4-week interlude across a compressed academic schedule should be deliberately extended by one or two additional weeks before Going Out begins.

## H.2 — Cohort membership runs from Going Deeper Wk 1 through Going Out Wk 12 as one unit

Cohorts are formed at Going Deeper Wk 1 and persist through Going Out Wk 12. Each cycle’s cohort is independent. Participants may join a new cycle as Round 2 alumni or as new participants entering Going Deeper, but cohort membership does not transfer mid-cycle.

This means: a Going Deeper cohort that begins in September completes its full arc into the Going Out series and finishes the following spring. A different cohort that begins Going Deeper in February completes its arc into the Going Out series the next academic year. The two cohorts never overlap as a single body. Each cycle’s formation arc is its own.

The implication for team rotation: each Companion’s commitment is one full Going-Deeper-plus-interlude-plus-Going-Out cycle (approximately 28–32 weeks), with at minimum one full cycle of rest before re-engaging as Lead or Cohort Companion. A team of 6 Companions covers two parallel cohort streams; a team of 9 covers three.

The implication for participant on-ramp: when continuous-run cohorts are offered (more than once a year), the Pre-Cohort 1:1 Discernment Conversation Guide handles new-participant intake at the rhythm of each new cycle’s start, not annually.
