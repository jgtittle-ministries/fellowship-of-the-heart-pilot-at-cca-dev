# **Quick Reference Card**

## WEEK 2 — THE SOILS RE-EXAMINED (90 minutes)

**Aim.** Engage the four soils as a working diagnostic of the participant’s current interior. The point is not which soil-type are you; the point is which soils are operating right now in which specific places of your life, and what is each blocking?

**Anchor scripture.** Mark 4:14–20 read in full; Jeremiah 17:9 (“the heart is deceitful above all things”) as the harder companion.

**Connect focus.** Self (diagnostic, deepened). The diagnostic block of Going Deeper begins tonight; Wk 3 (knots and lies) extends it; Wk 5 (confession) is the corresponding clearing work.

**Mode.** Shared teaching first; SPLIT into cohort circles for the diagnostic exercise; MERGE for closing.

**Center.** Each participant maps four specific places in their current life onto the four soils. Not ‘I am rocky soil’ but ‘with my mom right now, hard path; with my work, thorny; with prayer, rocky; with my best friend, good soil.’ The discussion in cohort circles surfaces patterns.

**Between-session practice.** Sit with one of the four soil regions — the one that surfaced most clearly tonight — for 30 minutes this week. Journal what is producing the condition. No fixing; just diagnosing.

**IJH source.** Vol 2 Exp 1 (Heart Soil Diagnostic, 90% confidence in the IJH framework — the highest-confidence diagnostic law in Vol 2). Mark 4:14–20 with Jesus’ own interpretation. Jeremiah 17:9 as the deeper companion text.

## WATCH FOR (Week 2 specific risks)

- The personality-quiz reading. Some participants will arrive ready to identify themselves as a soil type — ‘I’m the rocky one.’ The teaching tonight specifically dismantles this. Each participant has all four soils in different places. The question is not which one; the question is where each one is operating.
- Performance of ‘good soil’. Returning veterans may want to report that the interlude produced more good soil. Maybe; maybe not. The diagnostic only works on honest data.
- The teen who maps too much hard-path soil onto their parent. ‘My dad is hard-path right now.’ Re-frame: tonight is your interior, not your parent’s. ‘Where IN YOU is hard-path soil right now — maybe with regard to your dad?’
- The parent who maps too much thorny soil onto their teen. Same redirect: where in you is the thorny soil operating?
- Genuine grief surfacing. The diagnostic naming — ‘with my friendship with X, hard-path right now’ — may surface grief about a relationship the participant didn’t realize was that hard. Receive without rushing.
- Jeremiah 17:9 mishandled. The verse is harsh; some participants will read it as condemnation. The teaching frames it as honest diagnostic, not despair.
- The new participant who feels behind on the language. Cohort Companion should specifically check on each new participant during the cohort circle work.

## CRISIS CONTINGENCIES (Week 2)

*Week 2 is moderate-risk. The diagnostic naming surfaces interior material the Getting Started kept lighter. Most of what surfaces tonight is workable in the cohort circle; some will need offline follow-up.*

**If a participant’s mapping surfaces a relationship in active crisis.** Receive without alarm; do not problem-solve in the room. The Cohort Companion stays with the participant briefly after the cohort circle. Lead Companion follows up offline within 48 hours.

**If a participant maps mostly hard-path soil across all four places.** This is data, not a crisis. The participant is in a season of widespread hardness. Receive specifically: ‘What is true is true. We have ten more weeks; the hard-path will not be the whole story.’ Watch for despair signals; pastoral 1:1 within the week if needed.

**If a participant becomes overwhelmed during the diagnostic exercise.** Stop the exercise for them. Cohort Companion sits with them; quiet, low pressure. ‘You don’t have to finish the worksheet tonight. You can sit with the question across the week.’

**If a participant’s mapping reveals trauma underneath what looked like ordinary soil hardness.** Section 6 protocol of the Going Deeper Handbook. Do not interpret in the room. Pastoral / clinical referral if welcomed.

# **Session at a Glance**

## **Why this session, this week**

Wk 1 re-installed the room. Tonight begins the working depth. The Getting Started’s Wk 2 introduced the four soils as a description of the heart — a useful metaphor, lightly held. Going Deeper Wk 2 takes the same parable and uses it as a working diagnostic. Vol 2 of IJH names the Heart Soil Diagnostic Law as one of the highest-confidence claims in the entire framework: 90% confidence. The four soils are not four kinds of people; they are four conditions present simultaneously in every heart, in different regions, often shaped by different relationships and contexts.

The teaching tonight is straightforward; the work is the diagnostic. Each participant identifies four specific places in their current life — four relationships, areas, situations — and names which soil condition is operating in each. The naming surfaces patterns the participant did not see before they wrote them down. ‘I thought I was rocky soil this season. Looking at my four places, I see I’m good soil with my best friend, hard-path with my mom, thorny at work, and rocky with prayer. So the question isn’t “why am I rocky?” — it’s “why is each region the way it is?”’

Jeremiah 17:9 is the harder companion text. ‘The heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately sick; who can understand it?’ The verse names why the diagnostic matters — we cannot diagnose ourselves accurately by feel; we have to look at specific data. The four-places mapping is the data. The Spirit is the one who illumines what we cannot see.

Pick four specific places in your current life. Not categories — specific places. ‘My marriage right now.’ ‘My work this season.’ ‘My friendship with [name].’ ‘My prayer life this month.’ For each, name which soil condition is dominant. ## **Block 3 — Mark 4:14–20 — The Parable as Diagnostic (7:13–7:30, 17 min)**

Read the passage in full. Then walk Jesus’ own interpretation. Re-frame from summer.

## Script (the read)

*“Tonight’s scripture is Jesus’ own interpretation of the parable of the sower. Listen.”*

*(Read Mark 4:14–20 from the physical Bible, slowly.)*

*“The sower sows the word. And these are the ones along the path, where the word is sown: when they hear, Satan immediately comes and takes away the word that is sown in them. And these are the ones sown on rocky ground: the ones who, when they hear the word, immediately receive it with joy. And they have no root in themselves, but endure for a while; then, when tribulation or persecution arises on account of the word, immediately they fall away. And others are the ones sown among thorns. They are those who hear the word, but the cares of the world and the deceitfulness of riches and the desires for other things enter in and choke the word, and it proves unfruitful. But those that were sown on the good soil are the ones who hear the word and accept it and bear fruit, thirtyfold and sixtyfold and a hundredfold.”*

— Mark 4:14–20 (ESV)

### **Teaching points (use any format — do not read this verbatim)**

• The Getting Started’s Wk 2 named the four soils as a heart metaphor. Tonight we use the parable the way Jesus uses it — as diagnostic. Notice his own interpretation: each soil is a way of receiving the word. The hard-path receives nothing; the rocky receives but cannot sustain; the thorny receives but is choked; the good soil receives, sustains, and bears fruit.

• The IJH framework reads the parable one step further: the four soils are not four kinds of people. They are four conditions present simultaneously in every heart, in different regions, often shaped by different relationships and contexts. From Vol 2 Exp 1: ‘A single heart contains all four soils, in different places, at different times.’

• This re-reading is supported by Jeremiah 17:9. (Brief read.) The heart is not transparent to itself. We cannot know our soil conditions by feel — we have to look at specific data. The four-places mapping tonight is the data.

• What each soil names diagnostically:

## The four soil conditions

**HARD PATH — the word does not enter at all. In a region of hard-path, you have stopped expecting God to speak; the part of your heart that handles that area has gone numb or armored. Often follows specific old hurts.**

**ROCKY — the word lands but does not take root. There is real warmth, real responsiveness, but the depth isn’t there. Often shows up in new relationships or new commitments that haven’t had time to root.**

**THORNY — the word lands and roots, but other things compete. Cares of the world; deceitfulness of riches; desires for other things. Often shows up in middle-life seasons where there is too much going on.**

**GOOD SOIL — the word lands, roots, and produces fruit — thirty, sixty, a hundredfold. Specific. Sustainable. Visible to others. Often shows up in places where someone has been faithfully present over time.**

• The diagnostic question is not ‘which soil are you?’ The diagnostic question is: ‘Pick four specific places in your life right now. Which soil condition is operating in each?’ The honest answer is almost always all four, distributed.

• Why this matters. The clearing work of the rest of Going Deeper — the knot work of Wk 3, confession of Wk 5, the tools of Vol 2 Exp 6 — each works on one or more of these soil conditions. Knowing which condition is operating in which region tells you which tool the Spirit is most likely going to use.

## **Block 5 — The Four-Places Mapping in Cohort Circles (7:35–8:13, 38 min)**

Each cohort circle works in parallel. The structure is identical.

### **Inside the cohort circle — Companion script**

## Opening (3 min)

*“We have thirty-five minutes. The first twenty are quiet. The next eighteen, we share.”*

*“Worksheet on your chair — H2.1. Four lines. For each line, name a specific place in your life right now. Specific. Not ‘my friendships’ — ‘my friendship with [name].’ Not ‘my work’ — ‘my work in this current season, this specific role, this specific stretch.’ Then for each, mark which soil condition is dominant.”*

*“Be honest. ‘Good soil’ is not a goal; it’s a description. ‘Hard path’ is not a confession; it’s data. The diagnostic only works on truthful data.”*

*“Twenty minutes alone. I’ll signal when we share.”*

## Quiet writing (20 min)

**Each participant works H2.1 alone. The Cohort Companion works it too — not performatively, but actually. Twenty minutes is generous on purpose; some participants will write the four places quickly and spend the rest of the time noticing patterns.**

*Watch for: a participant who is finished after 5 minutes and looks bored. Quietly: ‘Sit with the four places. Notice if any soil condition surprises you. Notice if any one of the four places isn’t getting honest data yet.’ Most participants under-do the exercise; the quiet time is the diagnostic.*

## Sharing round (15 min)

*“Time. Around the circle, one at a time. Name your four places — you can be vague about the people involved if you need privacy (‘a friendship,’ ‘a relative’). Name the soil condition for each. One sentence about what you noticed about the pattern across the four. About 90 seconds each. Pass anytime.”*

*(Cohort Companion goes first. Specific. Honest. Brief. The model matters: the level of specificity the Cohort Companion brings is the level the cohort will match.)*

### **Cohort Companion: when to intervene**

- If a participant’s mapping is generic (‘my relationships are mostly good soil’) — push for specificity: ‘Pick ONE specific relationship. Which condition is operating in that one?’
- If a participant maps mostly hard-path across all four places — receive without alarm. ‘What is true is true. We have ten more weeks; tonight you have data.’ Quietly note for offline follow-up.
- If a participant maps too much soil onto another person (‘my dad is hard-path right now’) — redirect: ‘Tonight is your interior. Where in YOU is hard-path soil right now — maybe with regard to your dad?’
- If a participant becomes overwhelmed during their share — stop them gently. ‘You don’t have to finish tonight. Sit with the question across the week.’ Cohort Companion stays close.
- If a new participant freezes when their turn comes — ‘You can pass and listen, or you can name just one of the four places. Both work.’ No pressure.
- If the cohort wants to coach each other (‘Have you tried \_\_\_\_\_?’) — redirect: ‘Tonight is diagnostic. We are not fixing yet. We are seeing.’

# **Differentiation by Cohort**

## **Junior teens (12–14)**

## Adjustments

- Junior version of H2.1 uses simpler language: ‘four places where life is happening for you right now.’ The four places might be: my best friend; my mom or dad; a class at school; my prayer life or church.
- Pair turns are not used tonight — the cohort circle works as a single circle for the share.
- Watch for: junior teens who can’t identify four distinct places. ‘Three is enough.’ Don’t force a fourth.
- Watch for: junior teens whose four places are all family. Affirm; gently invite them to consider one place outside family for the diagnostic. Family-only soil mapping can mask other regions.
- Watch for: junior teens who map everything as good soil. Performance. Push gently: ‘Of the four — which one is the LEAST like good soil right now?’ The least-like is often the diagnostic gold.
- Watch for: the junior teen whose mapping reveals hard-path with a parent. Receive without alarm; do not interpret in the cohort circle. Cohort Companion follow-up offline within the week.

## **Senior teens (15–18)**

## Adjustments

- Senior version of H2.1 uses fuller IJH language. Four places might include: a friendship; a romantic interest; school or college decisions; faith life; family-of-origin.
- Watch for: the senior whose mapping is intellectually polished but emotionally remote. ‘Where in this is YOU — not the analysis of you?’
- Watch for: the senior whose mapping reveals significant rocky soil in romantic relationship territory. Receive without coaching.
- Watch for: the senior who realizes mid-exercise that their friendship-soil is mostly performance. The realization is the work.
- Watch for: the senior in college decision season who maps everything onto vocational territory. Re-frame: ‘What about places that aren’t about your future career?’

## **Parents**

## Adjustments

- Parent version of H2.1 includes prompts for adult life-stage. Four places might include: marriage; one specific friendship; vocation; faith life; family-of-origin; or a current relational tension.
- Watch for: the parent whose mapping reveals that one major place — marriage, vocation, parenting an adult child — has been hard-path soil for years and has been suppressed. Receive without rushing. Pastoral 1:1 within the week if welcomed.
- Watch for: the parent who maps thorny everywhere. Mid-life often reads as thorny because mid-life IS structurally thorny — too many cares competing. Affirm; gently invite the question of which thorn is most worth addressing.
- Watch for: the parent whose mapping centers on their teen-in-another-room. Re-frame: ‘Where in YOUR interior is this soil — not in your teen.’
- Watch for: the parent who maps good soil with their spouse and rocky everywhere else. Often a season-of-life pattern; affirm without minimizing.

# **Companion Debrief Prompts**

Companion team meets the day after Week 2 for a 45-minute debrief.

### **Signs the session worked**

- Each cohort circle had at least one participant whose mapping surprised them — a soil condition they didn’t see coming.
- The mappings showed range. If everyone in a cohort produced the same pattern, the cohort is performing rather than diagnosing.
- At least one participant in each cohort named a specific hard-path region honestly.
- The Cohort Companion’s own mapping (modeled at the start of the share) was specific and honest — not generic.
- The merge surfaced patterns that were not obvious before the cohort circles.

### **Signs the session did not work as well as it could have**

- Mappings were generic. ‘My relationships are mostly good’ with no specifics.
- Most cohort participants mapped everything as good soil.
- The teaching block ran long and the cohort circle ran short.
- A participant left visibly distressed without being checked on.
- New participants felt sidelined in their cohort circles.

### **If the session did not work — what to adjust for Week 3**

- If the diagnostic was generic, Wk 3 can re-anchor: ‘Last week’s diagnostic was the soil; this week’s is what is producing the soil condition. Specificity is the practice.’
- If new participants felt sidelined, the standing pair partner assignment in Wk 3 is the structural fix — each new participant gets a returning veteran partner.

### **People to follow up with this week**

- Anyone who mapped mostly hard-path soil. Warm contact within the week.
- Anyone whose mapping revealed a relationship in active crisis. Pastoral 1:1 within the week if welcomed.
- Any teen whose mapping revealed hard-path with a parent. Cohort Companion follow-up offline.
- Any parent whose mapping revealed long-term hard-path with marriage or vocation. Pastoral 1:1 within the week if welcomed.
- Any new participant who struggled with the exercise. Brief encouragement; offer to walk through it 1:1 if helpful.

**Handout H2.2 — The Four Soils Reference Card**

*From IJH Vol 2 Exp 1 (Heart Soil Diagnostic). Carry this card; it will be useful across Going Deeper.*

## HARD PATH

**The word does not enter at all. In a region of hard-path, you have stopped expecting God to speak; the part of your heart that handles that area has gone numb or armored. Often follows specific old hurts that were never named or addressed.**

*Diagnostic question: Where have I stopped expecting?*

## ROCKY

**The word lands but does not take root. There is real warmth, real responsiveness, but the depth isn’t there. Often shows up in new commitments that haven’t had time to root, or in relationships where surface affection has not yet built underground structure.**

*Diagnostic question: Where am I responding without rooting?*

## THORNY

**The word lands and roots, but other things compete. Cares of the world; deceitfulness of riches; desires for other things. Often shows up in middle-life seasons where there is too much going on.**

*Diagnostic question: What is choking what should be growing here?*

## GOOD SOIL

**The word lands, roots, and produces fruit — thirty, sixty, a hundredfold. Specific. Sustainable. Visible to others. Often shows up in places where someone has been faithfully present over time.**

*Diagnostic question: What is bearing fruit, and what made the soil ready?*

## Three things to remember

- These are not four kinds of people. They are four conditions present in every heart, in different places, often shaped by different relationships and contexts.
- The diagnostic does not produce a verdict; it produces data. Hard-path is not condemnation; it is information.
- The Spirit is the agent of change. The diagnostic surfaces the regions; the clearing work of the rest of Going Deeper (knot work, confession, hearing prayer) is the Spirit’s work using the tools.
