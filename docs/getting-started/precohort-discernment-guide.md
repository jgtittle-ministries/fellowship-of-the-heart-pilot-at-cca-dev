# Purpose

This guide structures a 30-minute conversation between an inviting Companion and an applicant — an individual or a parent-and-teen pair — held 4 to 8 weeks before Getting Started Wk 1.

The conversation has four aims:

- Surface the applicant’s specific reasons for considering the cohort.
- Test the fit between what the applicant wants and what Getting Started is.
- Honor decline as a real option — architecturally protect non-applicants from feeling shamed.
- Set practical expectations about time commitment, container conditions, and the long obedience that begins after the formal series.

# Before the Conversation

## Setting

Hold the conversation in a third-place setting — a coffee shop, a quiet office, a porch. NOT a classroom, NOT a church boardroom, NOT the school. The setting communicates that this is a personal discernment conversation, not an interview.

## Who is present

For an individual adult applicant: just the applicant and the inviting Companion.

For a parent-and-teen pair: BOTH pair members AND the inviting Companion. The pair’s pre-cohort dynamic is itself part of the discernment; do not split the conversation into separate parent and teen meetings.

## What to bring

- This guide (printed).
- A small notebook for brief notes after the conversation. Do not take notes during.
- Nothing else. No information packets, no curriculum outlines, no testimonials. The conversation is the only material.

## How the Companion prepares

Twenty minutes before the conversation, the Companion prays briefly: ‘Lord, you know what is alive in this person/pair right now. Help me listen for it. Help me name what Getting Started honestly is. Help me hold the discernment lightly. The Spirit is the One who calls; I am the one who invites.’

Read this guide once before each conversation. Even the third or fourth time, the questions land differently each time depending on the applicant; rehearsing keeps the conversation fresh.

# Block 1 — Listening (12 minutes)

The Companion asks four questions and listens. The applicant answers each in 1–3 minutes. The Companion’s discipline: ask, listen, brief reflection back, move to the next question. Do NOT interpret, do NOT solve, do NOT teach in this block.

## The four questions

## QUESTION 1.

*‘What do you sense the Spirit might be doing in you right now?’*

If the question lands and the applicant answers from interior specificity, you have your most important data point. If the applicant deflects (‘I haven’t really thought about it’ / ‘I just want to grow spiritually’), do not push; the deflection is also data.

For parent-and-teen pairs, ask each member separately within this question. Parent first, teen second. The teen’s answer is its own data; resist the temptation to coach the teen.

## QUESTION 2.

*‘What’s drawing you toward something like this?’*

‘Something like this’ — not ‘toward our cohort.’ The phrasing protects the applicant from feeling they have to validate the program. Listen for the moment they describe what they’re hungry for; that moment is the architecturally important data.

Watch for: ‘my friend recommended you’ (relational not interior); ‘the school recommended it’ (institutional not interior); ‘we want something deep’ (vague). None of these disqualify; all of them invite the next question to find specificity.

## QUESTION 3.

*‘What are you a little afraid of?’*

This is the most important of the four questions. The applicant who answers honestly here has done part of the discernment work already. The applicant who deflects (‘I’m not afraid; I’m excited’) needs gentle persistence: ‘Even a little? Most people walking into something like this are a little afraid of something. What is it for you?’

Common honest answers: being asked to share more than I want to; being judged; the time commitment; the teen feeling forced; the parent finding out something difficult. All of these are healthy starting fears.

## QUESTION 4.

*‘What would you NOT want this to be?’*

This question surfaces the applicant’s history. ‘I don’t want it to be like the small group I left.’ ‘I don’t want it to be a Bible study where we read and don’t talk.’ ‘I don’t want it to feel like therapy.’ ‘I don’t want my teen to be put on the spot.’

Each ‘not’ is information about what the applicant is bringing in. Some of it Getting Started can honor architecturally; some of it the applicant will need to know about Getting Started’s real shape so they can decide honestly.

## Companion discipline in Block 1

- Listen 80% of the time. The Companion’s words during this block: the questions themselves, brief reflection-back (‘what I heard you saying is \_\_’), and the move to the next question.
- No interpretation. ‘It sounds like the Lord is doing X’ — don’t. The applicant’s framing is the data; interpreting it changes what is heard.
- No fixing. If the applicant names a fear, the Companion does not say ‘Don’t worry, the cohort will protect you.’ The fear is data; the Companion holds it.
- Honor pauses. Silence after a question is not awkwardness; it is the applicant working. Wait.
- Brief reflection-back is the discipline. ‘What I heard you saying is that you’re drawn to the depth but a little afraid of being seen too clearly. Did I hear that right?’ The reflection-back gives the applicant a chance to refine.

# Block 2 — Naming (12 minutes)

Now the Companion names what Getting Started is, in a specific architectural way. Three things to name; do not add. Do not improvise outside this list. The architecture is what makes the cohort the cohort; vague descriptions produce vague applicants.

## The three things to name

## ONE — The four Connects as the spine.

‘Getting Started’s fifteen weeks walk through what the Intentional Journey of the Heart calls the four Connects: Self, Others, God, and Mission. Each Connect gets its own stretch of weeks, and by the closing sessions we’ve touched all four together. The four Connects aren’t a checklist — they’re the architecture of how Scripture says formation actually happens.’

Brief enough; do not over-explain. The applicant doesn’t need to understand the four Connects fully tonight; they need to know that the cohort has a spine.

## TWO — The container conditions as the working culture.

‘Each session has four container conditions — Safe, Present, Clear, Intentional. Safe means what is named in the room stays in the room. Present means phones go in a basket and we’re actually here. Clear means we’re honest about what is and isn’t happening. Intentional means we’re here for specific work, not generic spiritual fellowship. The container is what makes the conversation real.’

Naming the container often produces relief in applicants who have been in groups that lacked it. Watch the applicant’s body language; this naming sometimes lands as the moment they decide yes.

## THREE — The kitchen-table specificity as the working tone.

‘Getting Started uses what we call kitchen-table language — specific, honest, slow. We don’t spiritualize. If something happened to you this week that mattered, we ask what specifically happened, not what God was teaching you through it. The specificity is what does the work.’

If the applicant looks puzzled by this, briefly give one example: ‘Last week’s session, someone was telling us about being frustrated with their teenager. Instead of saying ‘the Lord is teaching me patience,’ they described the specific Tuesday afternoon when their teen slammed a door and what they wanted to say back. That’s the specificity. The Spirit does the patience work; we do the noticing.’ (Adapt example as appropriate.)

## And what Getting Started is NOT

After naming the three things, the Companion names three things Getting Started is not. Brief; honest; protects against scope-creep:

- Not therapy. The Companions are not licensed clinical professionals (unless they are; in which case they are still not acting clinically in the cohort). If clinical issues surface, we refer.
- Not a Bible study. We engage Scripture, but the spine is formation through the four Connects, not text-by-text Bible study.
- Not a program with a measurable outcome. The Spirit forms; we facilitate. Specific outcomes — marriage healing, vocational clarity, teen breakthrough — may come; none are guaranteed and none are the goal.

## Practical expectations

Before closing Block 2, the Companion names the practical commitments:

- Ten Tuesday evenings, 90 minutes each (Wk 10 is 120 minutes — the Family Commissioning).
- About 15 minutes a day between sessions for a journal practice.
- One pre-cohort conversation — this one. One Family Pre-Cohort Conversation Worksheet to complete with your pair partner the week before Wk 1.
- After Getting Started, Going Deeper in the next series cycle is invited but not assumed. Many participants do Getting Started and stop there; that is honest and good. The architecture honors both continuing and not.

# Block 3 — Discerning (6 minutes)

The Companion and applicant discern together whether the cohort is the right next step for THIS specific applicant in THIS specific season. Three honest options are named; the Companion does not pressure toward yes.

## The three options

## YES — AND READY.

The applicant senses this is the right cohort in the right season. The Companion notes their interest; the team will follow up by [specific date] with a formal invitation.

Companion language: ‘It sounds like this is right for you in this season. The team meets together this week to confirm the cohort’s composition; you’ll hear from us by [date].’

## YES — BUT NOT YET.

The applicant senses the cohort is for them, but not in this season. A specific reason — a season of family transition, a vocational pressure, a health season, a teen who isn’t ready. The Companion holds this season with them; the door is open for a future cohort cycle.

Companion language: ‘What you’re describing sounds real. This season isn’t the right season for the cohort. We hold this season with you. When you sense the timing has shifted, please let us know; we run cohorts again [next cycle date if known], and we’ll start the conversation again then.’

## NO — AND HONEST.

The applicant senses the cohort isn’t for them. The architecture, the depth, the time commitment, the format — something specific. The Companion honors this; declining is a real option, not a disappointment.

Companion language: ‘What you’re telling me is honest, and I want to thank you for that honesty. The cohort isn’t for everyone; honest no protects you and protects the cohort. We continue to walk with you in the ways we already walk; nothing changes between us. If something shifts in a future season, the door is open then.’

## Companion discipline in Block 3

- Do not pressure toward yes. The applicant’s capacity to discern honestly depends on the Companion’s capacity not to pressure.
- Do not over-celebrate yes. The applicant’s yes is for them, not for the Companion’s recruitment count. ‘We’re glad you’re considering this’ is the right temperature; ‘that’s wonderful, you’re going to love it’ is too high.
- Do not under-honor no. ‘We’ll miss you’ implies the applicant was inadequate. ‘We hold this season with you; the door is open’ honors the no without subtle disappointment.
- Do not promise outcomes. Even at the moment of yes, the Companion does not promise transformation, healing, or breakthrough. Vol 2’s confidence-percentage discipline applies: the work is real, the Spirit is faithful, and the specific shape of what is formed is the Spirit’s domain, not ours.

## If the applicant is uncertain at the close of Block 3

Some applicants will not be able to choose among the three options in 6 minutes. This is honest. The Companion does not press; instead, names that uncertainty is itself information:

‘What you’re describing sounds like ‘I’m not sure yet.’ That’s honest. Take a week. Pray. Talk to [pair partner / spouse / pastor]. Let me know by [specific date] one of the three: yes-and-ready, yes-but-not-yet, or no-and-honest. There’s no penalty for any of them. I’ll wait for what is honest.’

# After the Conversation

## Companion notes (within 24 hours)

Within 24 hours, the Companion writes 1–2 paragraphs of notes for the team’s discernment. Notes cover:

- The applicant’s name and the date of the conversation.
- The Block 1 listening data — what the four questions surfaced. Specific, not summary.
- The Block 2 naming response — what landed, what didn’t, what the applicant questioned.
- The Block 3 discernment outcome — yes-ready, yes-but-not-yet, no-honest, or uncertain (with the by-date).
- The Companion’s pastoral read — honest assessment of fit. Brief and specific.

## Team triage

The Companion team meets weekly during the recruitment window to triage 1:1 outcomes. The team’s questions:

- Of the yes-and-ready applicants, who is the cohort composed of? The team’s decision is about the cohort as a whole, not individual yes/no.
- Are there yes-but-not-yet applicants the team should hold for the next cycle?
- Any pastoral concerns the conversations surfaced — acute material, family dynamics, relational ruptures — that need attention regardless of the cohort decision?

The decision to extend a formal invitation is the team’s, not the inviting Companion’s. The inviting Companion’s pastoral judgment is weighted but not unilateral.

## Following up with the applicant

Within one week of the conversation:

- Yes-and-ready applicants receive the formal invitation with cohort dates, the Family Pre-Cohort Conversation Worksheet, and the next-step logistics.
- Yes-but-not-yet applicants receive a brief warm note thanking them for the honest discernment and noting the next cohort cycle’s timing.
- No-and-honest applicants receive a brief warm note thanking them; no recruitment language about future cycles unless they raised it themselves.
- Uncertain applicants receive nothing until their by-date; the Companion follows up only if they don’t respond by the agreed-upon date, and only with a one-line message: ‘Hi [name] — just confirming, did you settle on a discernment? No pressure either way; just want to make sure I don’t lose your reply.’

# Adaptations

## For an individual adult applicant (no parent-and-teen pair)

Same architecture; same four Block 1 questions; same Block 2 naming. Block 3 unchanged.

In Block 1, the questions are answered by one person rather than two. Conversation typically runs the full 30 minutes; rarely shorter.

## For a parent-and-teen pair

Both pair members present throughout. In Block 1, each member of the pair answers each question separately; parent first, teen second, by convention.

Watch for: the parent who answers the teen’s questions for them. Gently redirect: ‘Let’s hear from [teen’s name] in their own words.’

Watch for: the teen who defers entirely to the parent. Honor the deference but ask one focused question directly: ‘What’s one thing you’d hope this is, in your own words?’

In Block 3, both pair members must arrive at the same discernment for yes. If the parent says yes-ready and the teen says no-honest, the cohort architecture honors the teen’s no — a teen forced into the cohort is a teen who will disengage by Wk 3. The conversation closes with no-and-honest; the door for future cycles is open.

## For a CCA-internal applicant (current student’s family)

No major adaptation; the school connection is implicit. The Companion may briefly acknowledge the connection (‘This is hosted at CCA, but Fellowship of the Heart is not a school program in the same way classes are; it’s a parallel formation cohort’) without dwelling on it.

## For a non-CCA applicant

Add brief framing in Block 2: ‘Although Fellowship of the Heart is hosted at CCA, the cohort welcomes participants from beyond the school community when the fit is right. Many of you won’t have a school connection at all; that’s honest and fine. The cohort’s spine is the four Connects, not the school.’

# Common Situations and Pastoral Notes

## THE OVER-EAGER APPLICANT.

‘I’m so ready for this. I’ve been waiting my whole life for this. When can I start?’

Receive warmly without matching the energy. Over-eagerness sometimes signals that the applicant has been in many programs and brings a program-shopper posture. Hold the architecture: ‘We’re glad you’re considering it. Block 3 is still where we discern — take the week if you need it.’ Watch how they handle the discipline of slowing down.

## THE RELUCTANT TEEN.

Teen attends because the parent set up the conversation. Visible reluctance.

Honor the reluctance; don’t push past it. Block 1 Q3 (‘What are you a little afraid of?’) often unlocks honest engagement. If the teen remains shut down, name it kindly: ‘It sounds like you’re here mostly because [parent] asked you to be. I want you to know two things: it’s okay to say no tonight without consequences, and the cohort works only if you’re honestly in.’ The teen’s yes must be free.

## THE APPLICANT IN ACUTE CRISIS.

The conversation surfaces material indicating the applicant is in active crisis (mental-health, marriage, vocational rupture, recent loss).

The cohort is not the right venue for acute crisis. Pastoral care is. Companion language: ‘What you’re describing is real and is bigger than what the cohort architecture is built to hold. Let’s walk through what pastoral support is available to you right now — [specific names, contact paths]. The cohort can be a future season; this season needs different support.’ Refer pastorally and clinically as appropriate; the cohort discernment is a clean no for this season.

## THE APPLICANT WITH PRIOR HARM IN A SIMILAR PROGRAM.

‘I tried something like this before and it was harmful.’

Honor the harm; don’t defend the cohort. Listen specifically to what the prior program did wrong. Often the harm was about boundary violations, leader pressure, or theological coercion — things Getting Started’s container architecture is designed to prevent. The Companion can name this without promising it: ‘What you’re describing is real, and the way the cohort is built tries to protect against exactly that. I can’t promise it won’t happen here, but I can name how the architecture is different. If you decide to consider it, do so carefully — take the full week to discern, and you can step out at any time without penalty.’

## THE APPLICANT WHO ASKS FOR REFERENCES.

‘Can I talk to people who’ve done this before?’

Reasonable request. The Companion does NOT give names without prior consent from those participants. Standard response: ‘Yes — with their permission. I’ll ask two former participants whether they’d be open to a brief conversation with you, and connect you if they say yes. Give me 48 hours.’

## THE APPLICANT WHO ASKS ‘IS THIS BIBLICAL?’

Honest theological question. Brief honest answer: ‘Yes — the four Connects are drawn from Scripture’s description of formation; the container conditions are drawn from Vol 2 of the curriculum, which works through Scripture’s account of how God forms persons in community. Each session’s anchor scripture sets the architecture. The curriculum is denominationally light and theologically Trinitarian; if you have specific theological concerns, I’m happy to walk through them.’ If they have specific concerns, take the time to engage; the conversation may extend past 30 minutes for this kind of substantive theological dialogue.

# A Closing Note for the Companion

This conversation is a discipline. The first three or four times you hold it, you will overshoot in some direction — too much teaching in Block 2, too much pressure in Block 3, too little patience in Block 1. The architecture absorbs this; the applicant’s honest answers correct your overshoot.

Two things to remember as you become more skilled at the conversation:

ONE — The Spirit is the One who calls. You are the one who invites. Most of the work of discernment happens between the conversation and the applicant’s answer one or two weeks later. Your job is to make the architecture clear, not to produce the discernment.

TWO — The cohort’s integrity matters more than any individual yes. A cohort of 8 people who said yes honestly is a stronger cohort than a cohort of 12 people where 4 said yes under pressure. Resist the temptation to fill spots.

Take notes after each conversation. Bring them to the team. Refine the architecture each cycle. The conversation that you hold three years from now will land more cleanly than the one you hold this week, and the cohort will be the stronger for it.
