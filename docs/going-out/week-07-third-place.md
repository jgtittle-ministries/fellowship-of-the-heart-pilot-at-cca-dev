## WEEK 7 — THE THIRD-PLACE NETWORK (90 minutes)

**Aim.** Open the mission-far block. Surface the past week’s third-place / wider-context Tells. Engage the cohort with the architecture of the third-place network as a missional terrain. ## WATCH FOR (Week 7 specific risks — read twice)

*Tonight’s risks center on the conceptual transition from close-to-home to mission far, the temptation to inflate third-place engagement into formal mission, and the comparison dynamics between members with rich third-place networks and those without.*

**Inflation. A member treats their third-place network as the missional answer when it is actually the bridging terrain. Re-frame: third-place is the practice ground where the mission-far posture forms; specific missional engagement (Wk 8) is the next layer.**

**Hollow third-place. A member realizes they have almost no third-place network — work and family consume their hours, the rest of life is digital and isolated. Receive cleanly. The realization is itself data; not all spring members will have rich third-place to engage. Pastoral 1:1 if welcomed.**

**Church-as-third-place confusion. A member treats their church community as third-place, when church for them is closer to second-family. Distinguish carefully: third-place is the network that is neither household nor vocational. The architectural significance is that the third-place is the cohort’s structural counter to gnostic-community closedness (Vol 2 Exp 9): it is what holds the cohort’s discernment honest by keeping the body in contact with the broader world; the local church may or may not function as third-place depending on the member’s relationship to it.**

**Comparison shame. A member with a thin third-place network hears another member’s thick neighbourhood + hobby + community-board landscape and feels behind. Lead Companion frames at the start: third-place texture varies by life-stage, by vocational demand, by family configuration; comparison is not the work.**

**The third-place receiver in active crisis. A member’s Tell-receiver from the past week is in active crisis (a neighbour navigating divorce, a hobby-group friend in mental-health crisis, a fellow believer in vocational rupture). The Spirit may have placed the member in proximity for sustained engagement; receive cleanly; pastoral support for the member who is now carrying weight.**

**Politicized third-place content. The third-place network often surfaces political and culturally-charged conversation. The cohort’s discipline is the witness posture, not the political position. Receive contributions; do not adjudicate content.**

**Cross-cohort third-place exposure. A teen’s third-place network may overlap with a parent’s (same neighbours; same hobby groups; same church). Use indirect language; cross-cohort awareness held privately by Companions.**

**The downhill-uphill confusion. From Vol 2: most members default to uphill mission language (‘what should I DO?’) rather than downhill (‘what does my presence in the third-place produce already?’). Tonight’s teaching introduces the distinction; Wk 8 will use it directly.**

**Boundary uncertainty. A member’s third-place engagement raises questions about how much time, energy, or sustained attention to give a specific receiver. Pastoral 1:1; Going Out will not adjudicate boundary questions in the cohort space.**

## CRISIS CONTINGENCIES (Week 7)

*Crisis risk is moderate. Third-place engagement occasionally surfaces material requiring pastoral or clinical support.*

**If a third-place receiver is in active mental-health, relational, or safety crisis: receive briefly without making the cohort the venue; pastoral 1:1 within 24 hours; clinical referral as appropriate; the member may need supported exit if the receiver’s situation crosses what a non-clinical witness can hold.**

**If a teen’s third-place context (hobby group, sports team, social network) reveals abuse or exploitation: Section 6 protocol; mandatory-reporting law applies; school-level or institutional conduct processes engaged.**

**If a member’s third-place engagement has produced a relational opening that has now grown beyond what the member is equipped to hold (a neighbour disclosing abuse history; a hobby-group friend in suicidal ideation): receive the member’s honest report; pastoral support for the member; clinical referral for the receiver.**

**If a member’s third-place network reveals their own social isolation in a way that crosses into mental-health concern: pastoral 1:1; the third-place practice may need to be paused for direct attention to the member’s own wellbeing.**

**If politicized third-place content surfaces in a way that breaks the cohort’s peace (a Tell about a contentious political conversation that other cohort members react to): Lead Companion redirects to the architectural question (the witness posture) without adjudicating content; pastoral conversations outside the session as needed.**

**Session at a Glance**

**Why this session, this week**

Going Out’s mission-close-to-home block (Wks 4–6) engaged the daily-Tells data, the household, and the vocational — the domains where most cohort members spend most of their hours. Mission far (Wks 7–9) engages the broader geography. The Acts 1:8 architecture: from Jerusalem (immediate) to Judea and Samaria (broader) to the ends of the earth (specific missional engagements). Wk 7 opens the broader geography; Wk 8 walks the cohort’s specific missional discernment; Wk 9 prepares for sending beyond Going Out.

The third-place network is the practical name for what sits between household / vocational and formal mission. Sociologist Ray Oldenburg coined ‘third-place’ for the social settings beyond home (first place) and work (second place) — cafes, neighbourhood gatherings, hobby groups, civic associations, religious communities engaged outside formal worship. Most witness in the New Testament happens in some form of third-place: the Jerusalem temple courts where Peter preaches; the marketplaces where Paul argues; the homes of receptive listeners; the prison where Paul writes; the road where Philip meets the Ethiopian eunuch.

Acts 8 is the architectural anchor for tonight. Philip’s Samaritan mission begins because persecution scatters the Jerusalem church into the broader geography (Acts 8:1–4); Philip’s encounter with the Ethiopian eunuch (8:26–40) shows the Spirit’s direction toward a specific receiver in a specific place. The pattern is mixed: persecution-driven scattering produces general witness; specific Spirit-direction produces particular encounters. Both modes operate; both are paradigmatic for tonight’s reflection.

Ephesians 2:10 grounds the work theologically: ‘we are his workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works which God prepared beforehand, that we should walk in them.’ The works were prepared. The cohort’s task is not to invent missional engagements but to discover what the Spirit has already prepared. Tonight’s teaching introduces Vol 2’s downhill / uphill distinction — the witness that flows from who you are when you are most yourself, vs. the witness you have to push for. Wk 8 will use the distinction directly.

**Teaching points**

**•** Notice what Acts 8 establishes. Two modes operate. ONE — persecution-driven scattering produces general witness (‘those who were scattered went about preaching the word’); the body-on-the-move spreads simply because it is moving. TWO — specific Spirit-direction produces particular encounters (Philip and the Ethiopian eunuch); the Spirit prepares specific receivers in specific places at specific times. Both modes operate in the cohort’s third-place witness this season.

**•** Notice what Eph 2:10 establishes. The works were prepared beforehand. Our task is to walk into what the Spirit has already laid out. This shifts the posture from ‘what should I DO for God in mission?’ to ‘where has God already prepared something for me to walk into?’ Going Out’s morning noticing question — ‘Lord, where might I already be sent that I have not noticed?’ — has been training this posture all season.

**•** Vol 2 names a distinction that helps tonight. Uphill mission is what you DO — it has a goal, plan, measurable steps. Downhill mission is what you ARE — the effect you have on people simply by being fully present and yourself when most aligned with the Spirit. Both are real; most cohort members’ third-place witness is heavier on downhill than they realize. Tonight’s data look will surface where each is operating.

• The downhill / uphill distinction is broader than third-place. Each of the directions the cohort's discernment may confirm — vocational deepening, household formation, third-place arc, one-to-one accompanying, group-starting, contemplative life — can be enacted downhill or uphill. Downhill: it flows from who the Spirit has formed you to be; the gift goes outward; the energy is sustainable across years. Uphill: you are pushing the same gift in the same direction, but against the grain of what the Spirit has actually formed; the energy depletes; the work draws people inward toward you rather than outward toward the kingdom. The shadow-mission test from Vol 2 Exp 5 applies to every direction, not just to leadership: ask the people who know you well which one they're watching. Going Out's discernment work — your individual Wk 3 sent-context, the cohort's Wk 8 corporate landing — is calibrated to the downhill direction, whatever its shape.

## The downhill / uphill distinction

**UPHILL MISSION — what you do. Goal-directed; planned; measurable. Most missional language is uphill. Necessary; productive; takes effort to climb toward.**

**DOWNHILL MISSION — what you are. The effect you have on people simply by being fully yourself when most aligned with the Spirit. Flows; doesn’t have to be pushed. ‘When you are most aligned, what does the room get to be?’ The answer is your downhill mission.**

**MOST WITNESS IS DOWNHILL. The morning gentle conversation with the neighbour, the instinctive presence at the hobby-group friend’s hard moment, the quiet integrity that the youth coach has watched for three seasons. These are downhill; they flow from who the body has become.**

**UPHILL IS NEEDED, BUT NOT FIRST. Uphill mission asked for before downhill is sustained becomes performance. Going Deeper’s formation was clearing blockages so the downhill could flow. Spring’s mission far is, in part, learning to recognize and steward the downhill that is already happening.**

*“Tonight’s data look will surface what your third-place network is producing. Some will be Spirit-prepared encounters (Acts 8 mode 2); some will be cohort-engineered initiatives. Both are real. We are noticing what is already happening, mostly downhill.”*

**Handout H7.2 — Acts 8 + Eph 2:10 Reference**

*The architectural anchors for mission far. Carry this card; the framework runs across Wks 7–9.*

*Now those who were scattered went about preaching the word. Philip went down to the city of Samaria and proclaimed to them the Christ.*

*— Acts 8:26–27 (ESV)*

*For we are his workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand, that we should walk in them.*

*— Ephesians 2:10 (ESV)*

**Two modes of mission far**

## Both operate; both are paradigmatic

**MODE 1 — SCATTERING. Those who were scattered went about preaching the word. The body-on-the-move spreads simply because it is moving. Most third-place witness is in this mode — the Christian is in the neighbourhood, the hobby group, the civic association, simply because life has put them there; the witness is the body’s ordinary presence in the broader context.**

**MODE 2 — SPIRIT-DIRECTION. Specific direction toward a specific receiver in a specific place. ‘Rise and go toward the south… to the road.’ Less common; usually flows out of mode 1 (Philip is in the broader geography because of scattering before he is given the specific direction). When the Spirit prepares a particular encounter, the body recognizes it because it has been practicing presence.**

**What Eph 2:10 establishes**

## The works were prepared beforehand. Mission is not invented; it is discovered.

**‘We should walk in them.’ Walking is the verb. Not running, not sprinting; walking. Sustainable pace. Long faithfulness.**

**‘His workmanship.’ The body’s formation precedes the works. Going Deeper formed; Going Out walks.**

**Distinguishing third-place from…**

## …CLOSE-TO-HOME (Wks 4–6). Household and vocational are domains where the receivers know your history. Third-place receivers know less of you; relationships are typically less consequential and less observed; the witness can grow over time without the weight of long history.

**…FORMAL MISSION (Wk 8). Cohort-level missional engagement is a specific shared work the body discerns. Third-place is the bridging terrain — the practice ground where the mission-far posture forms and the Spirit-prepared encounters emerge. Most third-place engagement remains personal, not cohort-level.**

**Handout H7.3 — Downhill / Uphill Distinction Card**

*From Vol 2’s Mission Connect teaching. The distinction reorients how the body holds mission far.*

**Uphill mission**

## WHAT IT IS. What you DO. Goal-directed; planned; measurable. ‘I want to help neighbours through the difficult winter; I will organize a meal-rotation among the 6 households on my block.’

**WHEN IT FITS. When the Spirit asks for organized work. When a specific need calls for specific structure. When the body’s downhill capacity has been growing and is ready to be deployed.**

**RISK. Performance. Uphill mission attempted before downhill is sustained becomes work-without-flow. The body burns out; the mission decays; the witness becomes about the project rather than the people.**

**Downhill mission**

## WHAT IT IS. What you ARE. The effect you have on people simply by being fully yourself when most aligned with the Spirit. Flows; doesn’t have to be pushed.

**THE TEST QUESTION. ‘When you are most alive and genuinely yourself — not performing, not managing your image — what do the people around you get to be? What does the room feel like?’ The answer is closer to your downhill mission than any mission statement.**

**WHEN IT FITS. Most ordinary days. Most ordinary contexts. The 80% of mission that does not require organization.**

**RISK. Invisibility. Downhill mission can be so woven into ordinary life that the body does not recognize it as mission. Tonight’s data look helps with the noticing.**

**Both together**

## DOWNHILL MAKES UPHILL POSSIBLE. The body that flows downhill can sustain uphill work; the body that has not learned downhill cannot.

**UPHILL DEPLOYS DOWNHILL. The body that has been flowing downhill in the third-place becomes recognizable; relationships develop; specific calls emerge; uphill engagement becomes possible.**

**MOST OF SPRING’S MISSION FAR IS DOWNHILL. The cohort’s ordinary presence in third-place networks is the dominant mission-far work. Uphill engagements (Wk 8’s cohort discernment may surface some) are the exception, not the rule.**
