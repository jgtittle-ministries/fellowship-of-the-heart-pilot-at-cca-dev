## WEEK 4 — THE DAILY TELLS (90 minutes)

**Aim.** Surface what the past three weeks of outside-cohort Tells have actually produced. Move from one-sentence cohort-circle reports (Wk 2) to substantive examination of patterns: what kinds of receivers; what kinds of moments; what kinds of cost; what kinds of fruit. The body looks at the body’s daily work and weighs what it sees. Refine the Tell practice for Wks 5–6 with specific differentiation by context.

**Anchor scripture.** John 13:35 (‘By this all people will know you are my disciples, if you have love for one another’); 1 Peter 3:15 (‘always being prepared to make a defense… yet do it with gentleness and respect’); Colossians 4:5–6 (‘Walk in wisdom toward outsiders, making the best use of the time. Let your speech always be gracious, seasoned with salt’).

**Connect focus.** Mission close to home. The daily relational world; the immediate sent-context; people each member already knows.

**Mode.** Whole-room. The cohort’s daily-Tells data is shared across cohort lines tonight; junior teens, senior teens, and parents all hear each other’s actual witness data. The cross-cohort visibility is the pedagogical centre.

**Center.** Brief teaching frame (15 min). Then a structured cohort survey: each member contributes ONE specific Tell from the past three weeks (not the most polished; the most honest), with brief data on receiver type, context, and what surfaced. Lead Companion captures patterns on flip chart in real time. Cohort weighs the patterns. Refinement for Wks 5–6 by domain (household, vocational, third-place).

**Between-session practice.** The weekly outside-cohort Tell continues, with a specific differentiation: this week’s Tell is in the HOUSEHOLD context (family-of-origin, present household, immediate domestic relationships). Daily noticing question shifts: ‘Where in my house am I a witness already, and where am I avoiding being one?’ Standing pair check-in this week: ‘What is the household witness asking of you specifically?’

**IJH source.** Vol 1 FL.XXI Household Formation Law (the Shema’s formation mechanism); Vol 2 Exp 9 (Community as Amplifier extended to outward witness); the ordinary-life witness reading of John 13:35 and 1 Peter 3:15.

## WATCH FOR (Week 4 specific risks — read twice)

*Tonight’s risks center on the comparison dynamics that surface when the cohort sees each other’s actual witness data side by side. Some members will feel exposed; some will compare themselves favorably or unfavorably; some will resist the cohort-level pattern surfacing.*

**Comparison and shame. A junior teen hears a parent’s vocational Tell and feels their school-friend Tell is small. A parent hears another parent’s costly family-of-origin Tell and feels their workplace Tell is shallow. The Lead Companion frames at the start: scale of context is not scale of faithfulness. The daily Tell to a sibling or classmate carries the same weight as a vocational Tell to a colleague.**

**Performative reframing. A member whose Tell did not happen reports it as if it did, or describes a non-Tell encounter as a Tell. The cohort’s honesty discipline has been built across Going Deeper and the first weeks of spring. The Lead Companion gently redirects when needed: ‘Honest data is the work. The cohort can hold a non-Tell.’**

**The cohort that has lapsed on the practice. If a substantial portion of the cohort has not done the weekly Tell three weeks running, tonight’s data will be thin. Receive this honestly; Going Out re-engages. The team’s discipline is to refuse to manufacture data that did not happen.**

**The Tell that produced unintended consequence. A member’s Tell three weeks ago has now produced a follow-on conversation, a relational shift, an unexpected opening, or a closing of a door. Receive cleanly; pastoral 1:1 within the week to walk what is unfolding.**

**The Tell that revealed the receiver’s active crisis. A friend mentioned in a Tell is now in active mental-health, relational, or safety crisis. The member is carrying this without being equipped to. Refer; pastoral 1:1; clinical referrals as appropriate. Mandatory-reporting law applies as relevant for teen receivers.**

**Cross-cohort family-Tell exposure. A teen’s Tell about their parent (in a different cohort) surfaces. A parent’s Tell about their teen surfaces. The architecture protects: initials only; the cross-cohort person does not enter the discussion in identifying detail; cross-cohort Companions hold the dynamic privately.**

**Theological flattening. A member frames every Tell as evangelism-toward-conversion (the Wk 2 distinction collapses). Re-frame: ‘We are surfacing the body’s daily witness, which includes evangelism but is broader. Tonight is the data, including non-evangelistic Tells.’**

**Politicized Tell content. A member describes a Tell that involved political content or culturally-charged conversation (Christian witness intersecting with current political debates). The architecture holds without adjudicating the political content; the cohort’s discipline is the witness posture, not the political position.**

## CRISIS CONTINGENCIES (Week 4)

*Crisis risk is moderate. The Tell-data review surfaces real-life material that occasionally crosses thresholds.*

**If a teen’s Tell-receiver is in active self-harm or crisis. Section 6 protocol immediately. Mandatory-reporting law applies where relevant. The teen Companion stays close to the teen for the rest of the session.**

**If a Tell revealed abuse or exploitation by an adult known to the cohort member. Mandatory-reporting law applies (Virginia CPS for minor victims; Virginia APS for vulnerable-adult victims). Two-adult rule for any reporting conversation. Pastoral 1:1 within 24 hours.**

**If a member’s Tell pattern reveals burnout in their witness practice (over-extended; exhausted; performing). Receive cleanly; sabbath-rhythm conversation in pastoral 1:1; the Tell practice itself may need to be paused for a week if the member is burning out.**

**If a Tell revealed marital or family rupture (the receiver was the member’s spouse and the Tell exposed long-standing material). Receive briefly; the cohort space is not the venue for marital adjudication; pastoral 1:1 with both partners if appropriate.**

**If the cohort’s data review reveals significant cross-cohort family material (a teen’s Tell of a parent, then the parent’s defensive response in their own contribution). Lead Companion holds the architecture; no adjudication in the cohort space; cross-cohort pastoral conversation outside the session.**

**Default. Section 6 of the Going Deeper Handbook v1.1 (carried forward).**

**Session at a Glance**

**Why this session, this week**

Going Out’s body-sent block (Wks 1–3) framed the architecture of being sent and produced each member’s working sent-context sentence. The mission-close-to-home block (Wks 4–6) makes the architecture concrete in the daily relational world. Wk 4 is the data look — what has the cohort actually been doing for the past three weeks of weekly outside-cohort Tells, and what does the data reveal about who the cohort is becoming as a sent body.

This is corporate-soul-clearing work in a particular form. Vol 2 proposes (in the open unknowns) a Community Soul Clearing Law — the corporate analog of inner healing. The cohort’s data review tonight is one shape of that work: the body looks honestly at the body’s daily life, names what it sees, weighs what is happening, and refines what is asked of itself going forward.

The cross-cohort visibility tonight is intentional. Wks 5 and 6 will split the cohort to engage household and vocational witness with developmentally-appropriate texture. But Wk 4 needs the body to see itself as one body — junior teens hearing what parents’ Tells look like, parents hearing what teens’ Tells look like, the senior cohort hearing both. The diversity of contexts is itself formation; the senior teen learns from the parent’s family-of-origin Tell that this kind of witness is also legitimate; the parent learns from the junior teen’s playground Tell that small daily witness counts.

The architectural discipline tonight is honest data over polished narrative. The Lead Companion captures patterns on the flip chart in real time — not interpretations, observations. ‘Receivers: 7 colleagues, 4 family members, 3 classmates, 2 neighbours, 1 service provider.’ ‘Tells that produced response: 4. Tells that produced silence: 6. Tells the member did not understand the receiver’s reaction: 5.’ The data is what it is; the cohort weighs what it means together.

**Block 2 — Three-Mode Teaching Frame (7:08–7:23, 15 min)**

## Script (the read)

*“Three passages frame what we are looking at tonight. Listen.”*

*By this all people will know you are my disciples, if you have love for one another.*

*— John 13:35 (ESV)*

*But in your hearts honor Christ the Lord as holy, always being prepared to make a defense to anyone who asks you for a reason for the hope that is in you; yet do it with gentleness and respect.*

*— 1 Peter 3:15 (ESV)*

*Walk in wisdom toward outsiders, making the best use of the time. Let your speech always be gracious, seasoned with salt, so that you may know how you ought to answer each person.*

*— Colossians 4:5–6 (ESV)*

**Teaching points**

**•** Three modes of witness emerge across these passages. RELATIONAL PRESENCE — John 13:35. The body’s love for one another that becomes visible to the world. The witness is who you are with people, not what you say. RESPONSIVE DEFENSE — 1 Peter 3:15. Someone asks; you answer with gentleness and respect. The witness is reactive; the receiver initiates. INTENTIONAL INITIATIVE — Colossians 4:5–6. The body walks in wisdom toward outsiders, making the best use of the time. The witness is proactive; the body moves toward the world.

**•** All three modes are real witness. Most of our daily Tells fall into one of these three categories. A common pattern: the cohort defaults to one mode without realizing the other two are also legitimate. The discipline tonight is seeing what mode each Tell uses, and noticing whether our cohort is using all three or just one.

**•** All three modes have characteristic risks. Relational presence can drift into withholding (the witness becomes invisible because nothing is ever said). Responsive defense can drift into reactivity (the witness only happens when triggered). Intentional initiative can drift into pressing (the witness becomes coercive).

## The three modes

**RELATIONAL PRESENCE. The body’s love made visible. ‘They will know’ by being WITH us — our love for one another, our integrity in ordinary life, our presence under suffering. The witness is woven into how we live. Risk: withholding (the witness becomes invisible).**

**RESPONSIVE DEFENSE. Someone asks; we answer. The witness is reactive but specific — the gentleness and respect matter; the answer matters; the timing is set by the receiver, not the witness. Risk: reactivity (the witness only happens when triggered).**

**INTENTIONAL INITIATIVE. The body walks toward outsiders — specifically, gracefully, with seasoned speech. The witness is proactive, with the wisdom to know how to answer each person. Risk: pressing (the witness becomes coercive).**

*“Tonight’s data look will reveal which modes our cohort uses, which we underuse, and what the patterns mean. We are not grading; we are seeing.”*

**Handout H4.2 — Three-Mode Witness Reference**

*Three modes of witness from the New Testament. All three count; all three have characteristic risks.*

**Mode 1 — Relational Presence**

## ANCHOR. John 13:35 — ‘By this all people will know you are my disciples, if you have love for one another.’

**WHAT IT IS. The body’s love made visible. The witness is who you ARE with people — your integrity in ordinary life, your presence under suffering, your faithfulness in small things.**

**WHEN IT FITS. Most ordinary days. Most ordinary contexts. The 80% of witness that does not require words.**

**RISK. Withholding. The witness becomes invisible because nothing is ever said. Words eventually need to be added; relational presence alone is incomplete.**

**Mode 2 — Responsive Defense**

## ANCHOR. 1 Peter 3:15 — ‘always being prepared to make a defense to anyone who asks you for a reason for the hope that is in you; yet do it with gentleness and respect.’

**WHAT IT IS. Someone asks; you answer. The witness is reactive but specific — the gentleness and respect matter; the answer matters; the timing is set by the receiver.**

**WHEN IT FITS. When the receiver initiates. When the question opens. When the moment arrives unprompted.**

**RISK. Reactivity. The witness only happens when triggered. The body that is only ever responsive may be hiding behind passivity.**

**Mode 3 — Intentional Initiative**

## ANCHOR. Colossians 4:5–6 — ‘Walk in wisdom toward outsiders, making the best use of the time. Let your speech always be gracious, seasoned with salt, so that you may know how you ought to answer each person.’

**WHAT IT IS. The body walks toward outsiders. Specifically. Gracefully. The witness is proactive; the body moves toward the world with seasoned speech.**

**WHEN IT FITS. When the Spirit moves you toward someone. When the time is right and the relationship is ready. When holding back would be unfaithful.**

**RISK. Pressing. The witness becomes coercive when the body forces its initiative beyond what the moment will hold. Salt seasons; it does not preserve by force.**

**Pattern questions for your own practice**

Which mode dominates your weekly Tells? Which mode is hardest for you? Which mode does the Spirit seem to be inviting more of in this season? Bring these questions to your standing pair.

**Handout H4.3 — Domain Differentiation Card**

*Three domains of close-to-home witness. Wk 5 engages household; Wk 6 engages vocational; Wk 7 begins mission far.*

**HOUSEHOLD (Wk 5)**

## WHO. Family-of-origin (parents, siblings, extended family). Present household (spouse, children, those you live with). Immediate domestic relationships.

**WHY HARD. The receivers know the witness’s history. Performance is impossible; the body has been seen for decades. The witness has to be honest about its own formation.**

**WHY CONSEQUENTIAL. The Shema’s formation mechanism happens here — ‘teach them diligently to your children, talk about them when you sit in your house.’ The household is the unit Vol 1’s proposed Household Formation Law engages.**

**VOCATIONAL (Wk 6)**

## WHO. Workplace colleagues (parents). School classmates and teachers (teens). Business partners, professional contacts, regular service relationships.

**WHY RECURRENT. This is where most members spend the bulk of their hours. The witness is repeated, observed, and accumulated over years.**

**WHY VISIBLE. The three modes are most visibly tested here — relational presence under deadlines, responsive defense when colleagues ask hard questions, intentional initiative when the Spirit opens a moment.**

**THIRD-PLACE (Wk 7 onward)**

## WHO. Neighbours, fellow church members outside the cohort, hobby groups, regular service providers, community connections that are not household and not vocational.

**WHY DIFFERENT. Lower stakes than household; lower frequency than vocational; the witness is often relationally specific without being deep.**

**WHY MISSIONALLY IMPORTANT. The third-place network is often where mission far emerges — a casual contact becomes a significant relationship; a neighbour becomes a friend; a hobby group becomes a context for sustained witness over years.**
